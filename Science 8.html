Welcome to Unit 1: Scientific Measurement & Practice — the foundation of all physical science. Every discovery in physics, chemistry, and engineering begins with careful, precise measurement. At St. Theresa School in Honolulu, our scientific investigations connect to the world around us — from measuring wave heights at Sandy Beach to tracking lava flow speeds at Kīlauea. Section 1: What Is Physical Science?

Physical science is the systematic study of matter and energy — the two fundamental components of the universe. Unlike life science (which studies living things) or earth science (which studies our planet's structure), physical science asks questions like: Why does a surfboard float? How does a ukulele make sound? Why does lava glow? How do rockets reach the Moon?

Physical science has two major branches. Physics studies forces, motion, energy, waves, and the fundamental laws governing how everything moves and interacts. Chemistry studies the composition, structure, and properties of matter and how substances change. At St. Theresa School, our 8th grade physical science course focuses primarily on physics — the laws that govern all matter and energy in the universe.

Physical science surrounds every aspect of Hawaiian life. The trade winds that cool our islands, the volcanic processes that built them, the ocean currents that carry the Hōkūleʻa across the Pacific, the solar panels that power our homes — all of these are physics in action. When you study physical science, you are learning to read the language of God's creation.

Section 2: The SI System — A Universal Language of Measurement

For centuries, different cultures used different measurement systems, causing confusion in trade and science. In 1960, the international scientific community adopted the Système International d'Unités (SI) — the modern metric system. Today, the SI system is used by scientists in every country and is the standard for all scientific communication worldwide.

The SI system is built on seven base units, each measuring a fundamental physical quantity. Every other unit in science (such as speed in m/s, force in Newtons, or energy in Joules) is derived from these seven base units.

Section 3: Accuracy, Precision, and Significant Figures

Two key concepts in scientific measurement are accuracy and precision, and understanding the difference is critical for evaluating data.

Accuracy refers to how close a measurement is to the true or accepted value. If a wave is actually 2.0 m tall and you measure it as 2.1 m, your measurement is quite accurate. If you measure it as 1.2 m, it is inaccurate.

Precision refers to how consistent and repeatable measurements are — even if they are wrong. If you measure the same wave five times and always get 1.2 m (even though the wave is 2.0 m), your measurements are precise but not accurate. If you get 1.2 m, 2.3 m, 1.8 m — your measurements are neither precise nor accurate.

Ideally, scientists want both accuracy and precision. In practice, systematic errors (like a poorly calibrated instrument) can make measurements consistently wrong (precise but inaccurate), while random errors produce inconsistent results (imprecise).

Significant figures (or "sig figs") tell us how precise a measurement is. Every digit in a properly reported measurement is significant. A measurement of 14.25 cm has four significant figures; 0.0045 m has two significant figures. When calculating with measurements, the answer should not have more significant figures than the least precise measurement used.

Example: You measure a surfboard length three times: 182 cm, 183 cm, 182 cm. Average = 182.3 cm. Your measurements are precise (consistent range). If the true length is 182 cm, they are also accurate. But if the true length is 195 cm (you used a warped ruler), they would be precise but NOT accurate.

Section 4: Scientific Notation and Very Large/Small Numbers

Physical science often deals with numbers that are inconveniently large (the speed of light: 299,792,458 m/s) or inconveniently small (the mass of a proton: 0.0000000000000000000000000016726 kg). Scientific notation provides a compact, standardized way to write such numbers.

In scientific notation, a number is written as a × 10ⁿ, where a is a number between 1 and 10 (one digit before the decimal), and n is an integer (positive or negative). To convert: move the decimal point until only one non-zero digit remains before the decimal; count the places moved (positive = moved left; negative = moved right).

Section 5: Measuring Tools and Techniques

Different measurements require different tools. Using the correct tool and technique is essential for obtaining reliable data.

  • Length: Meter stick or metric ruler (cm, mm precision); calipers (0.01 mm precision); laser rangefinders (used in surveying Hawaiian terrain).
  • Mass: Triple-beam balance (measures mass by comparison with known masses) or digital balance. Note: a scale measures weight (force), not mass directly.
  • Volume: Graduated cylinder for liquids (read at the bottom of the meniscus — the curved surface); beaker for approximate volumes; water displacement for irregular solids.
  • Temperature: Thermometer (in Celsius for science, Kelvin for thermodynamics); infrared thermometers (used to measure lava surface temperatures from a safe distance).
  • Time: Digital stopwatch, atomic clock, oscilloscope.

In 2018, the Kīlauea eruption produced lava flows measured in meters per hour, gas emissions in tons per day, and sulfur dioxide concentrations in parts per million.

Section 6: Data Organization — Tables and Graphs

Collecting measurements is only the beginning. Scientists must organize, display, and interpret data to find patterns and draw conclusions. Data tables organize information in rows and columns, with clearly labeled headers and units. Graphs reveal visual patterns in data.

Different types of graphs serve different purposes: Line graphs show how a variable changes continuously over time (like temperature throughout the day). Bar graphs compare separate categories (like rainfall at different Hawaiian locations). Scatter plots show the relationship between two variables. Pie charts show proportions of a whole.

All graphs must include: a clear title, labeled axes with units, an appropriate scale, a legend if multiple data sets are shown, and data points plotted accurately. In science, the independent variable (the one you control) goes on the x-axis, and the dependent variable (the one you measure) goes on the y-axis.

Section 7: Lab Safety at St. Theresa School

Every laboratory activity presents potential risks that can be managed through proper safety practices. At St. Theresa School, all students follow laboratory safety rules before, during, and after every experiment.

Essential safety rules: (1) Read all instructions completely before starting. (2) Wear appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE) — safety goggles, gloves, apron when working with chemicals. (3) Never eat, drink, or put anything in your mouth in the lab. (4) Report all accidents, spills, or injuries to the teacher immediately, no matter how minor. (5) Never perform unauthorized experiments. (6) Know the location of safety equipment: eyewash station, fire extinguisher, first aid kit, emergency shut-off. (7) Wash hands thoroughly before leaving the lab. (8) Handle glassware carefully; report broken glass immediately.

Lab safety is not just about following rules — it is about respecting the dignity and safety of yourself, your classmates, and your teacher. Careless behavior in a lab can cause serious, permanent injury. As stewards of the bodies God has given us, we have a responsibility to act wisely and carefully.

Unit 2

Motion and Forces

HCPS III: SC.8.8.1–SC.8.8.3  |  NGSS: HS-PS2-1  |  ⏱ 8 Weeks
Unit 2 brings us to the heart of classical physics: the study of motion and the forces that cause it. From a surfer catching a wave at Pipeline to a paddler driving an outrigger canoe through Ke Nui Channel, motion and forces are visible everywhere in Hawaiian life. The great Isaac Newton (1642–1727) gave us the three laws that describe motion precisely, revolutionizing science and technology. These laws govern everything from a coconut falling from a tree to a spacecraft traveling to Mars — and they are still taught and used exactly as Newton formulated them more than 300 years ago.

Section 1: Describing Motion — Distance, Displacement, Speed, and Velocity

Before studying forces, we must first understand how to describe motion precisely. Physics distinguishes carefully between quantities that seem similar but have important differences.

Distance is the total length of the path traveled, regardless of direction. If you walk around the entire perimeter of Ala Moana Beach Park, your distance traveled is the full perimeter length. Displacement is the straight-line distance from start to finish, measured in a specific direction. If you return to where you started, your displacement is zero — even though your distance traveled was significant.

Speed is how fast an object is moving — distance divided by time (v = d/t). Speed is a scalar quantity, meaning it has magnitude (a number and unit) but no direction. The average speed of a Hawaiian spinner dolphin is about 11 km/h; a racing outrigger canoe reaches 18–20 km/h with experienced paddlers.

Velocity is speed with direction — displacement divided by time, specifying both how fast and which way. Velocity is a vector quantity. "The canoe is moving at 15 km/h northeast" is a velocity statement. If a boat travels at 20 km/h south, then turns around and travels at 20 km/h north, its speed is the same, but its velocity has completely changed direction.

🌺

Hawaii Connection: Wave speed at open ocean can reach 800 km/h (as tsunamis), while near-shore surfing waves travel at 20–30 km/h. Lava flows range from 1 km/hr (thick 'a'ā lava) to 20+ km/hr (fast-moving pāhoehoe lava). These speeds all use the same formula: v = d/t.

Section 2: Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity — how quickly an object speeds up, slows down, or changes direction. The formula is: a = Δv / t (change in velocity divided by time). Acceleration is measured in meters per second per second, or m/s².

It is important to remember that acceleration occurs any time velocity changes — not just when an object speeds up. An object slowing down (decelerating) is also accelerating (negative acceleration). An object turning a corner at constant speed is also accelerating, because its direction of velocity is changing.

Near Earth's surface, gravity causes a free-fall acceleration of approximately 9.8 m/s² downward (often rounded to 10 m/s² for calculations). This means that a cliff diver falling off Waimea Bay gains 9.8 m/s of downward speed every second of free fall. After 1 second, she is falling at 9.8 m/s; after 2 seconds, at 19.6 m/s; and so on (ignoring air resistance).

Example: A surfer accelerates from 2 m/s to 8 m/s in 3 seconds as she catches a wave.
a = Δv / t = (8 - 2) m/s / 3 s = 6 m/s / 3 s = 2 m/s².

Section 3: Newton's First Law — The Law of Inertia

Isaac Newton's First Law of Motion states: An object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion continues in motion at the same speed and in the same direction, unless acted upon by an unbalanced (net) external force.

This property — the tendency of objects to resist changes in their state of motion — is called inertia. Inertia depends on mass: the more mass an object has, the more inertia it has, and the harder it is to start, stop, or change its direction.

You experience Newton's First Law constantly. When a bus suddenly brakes, you lurch forward — your body, obeying inertia, continues moving forward even as the bus stops. When a canoe hits a wave and suddenly decelerates, the paddlers continue forward momentarily. Seatbelts exist precisely because of inertia.

🌺

Hawaii Connection: A surfboard, once moving on a wave, will continue gliding after the wave passes until friction from the water and air drag slow it down (unbalanced forces). Hawaiian cliff divers rely on inertia to maintain a stable body position during free fall — absent any air resistance disruption, they continue falling in the same direction at increasing speed.

Section 4: Newton's Second Law — Force, Mass, and Acceleration

Newton's Second Law of Motion states: The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. This is expressed as the famous equation: F = ma (Force = mass × acceleration).

Force is measured in Newtons (N). One Newton is the force needed to accelerate a 1 kg object at 1 m/s². From F = ma, we can also rearrange to find acceleration (a = F/m) or mass (m = F/a).

The "net force" (Fnet) is the vector sum of all forces acting on an object. If forces are balanced (net force = 0), there is no acceleration — the object moves at constant velocity or stays at rest. If forces are unbalanced (net force ≠ 0), the object accelerates in the direction of the net force.

Faith Connection: Newton himself was a devout Christian who saw F = ma as evidence of God's rational design. He wrote: "This most beautiful system of the sun, planets, and comets could only proceed from the counsel and dominion of an intelligent and powerful Being." The elegant simplicity of F = ma — one equation describing all motion from a falling coconut to a spacecraft — is a remarkable testimony to the orderliness of creation.

Section 5: Newton's Third Law — Action and Reaction

Newton's Third Law of Motion states: For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force. This means forces always come in pairs — you cannot push on something without it pushing back on you with exactly the same force.

When a Hawaiian paddler pushes the paddle blade backward through the water (action force), the water pushes the paddle — and therefore the canoe — forward (reaction force). When you stand on the ground, gravity pulls you down (action), and the ground pushes up on you with an equal normal force (reaction). When a gun fires a bullet forward (action), the gun recoils backward (reaction).

It is crucial to understand that action-reaction pairs act on different objects. The paddle pushes the water; the water pushes the paddle. These are not balanced forces (which act on the same object) — they are Newton's Third Law pairs acting on separate objects.

🌺

Hawaii Connection: The Hōkūleʻa, the traditional Hawaiian double-hulled voyaging canoe, propels itself across the Pacific through action-reaction: paddles push water backward, water pushes canoe forward. Similarly, fish swim by pushing water backward with their tails; ocean birds like the albatross push air backward with their wings to generate forward flight — Newton's Third Law governs all of these.

Section 6: Gravity and Weight

Gravity is the fundamental attractive force between any two objects that have mass. The greater the masses and the closer together they are, the stronger the gravitational attraction. Near Earth's surface, gravity gives all objects a downward acceleration of 9.8 m/s² (often called g).

Weight is the gravitational force on an object: W = mg. Weight is a force measured in Newtons. Mass is the amount of matter in an object, measured in kilograms — it never changes regardless of location. A Hawaiian monk seal with a mass of 170 kg has a weight of 170 × 9.8 = 1,666 N on Earth. On the Moon (where g ≈ 1.6 m/s²), its weight would only be 272 N — but its mass remains 170 kg everywhere.

Friction is the contact force that opposes relative motion between surfaces. Friction can be helpful (allowing us to walk, drive, and surf without slipping) or harmful (slowing machines and requiring extra energy). Friction force depends on the types of materials in contact and the normal force pressing them together.

Unit 3

Rockets, Rotation & Momentum

HCPS III: SC.8.8.4–SC.8.8.6  |  NGSS: HS-PS2-1 to HS-PS2-3  |  ⏱ 8 Weeks
Unit 3 takes us into some of the most exciting territory in physical science: momentum, rockets, and rotation. These concepts explain how astronauts reach the International Space Station, how traditional Hawaiian stone slings worked, how figure skaters spin, and how the planets stay in their orbits. The principles in this unit are the same ones used by NASA engineers at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, by navigators aboard the Hōkūleʻa, and by physicists at the Mauna Kea Observatories — and they begin with a single elegant concept: momentum.

Section 1: Momentum — The Quantity of Motion

Momentum is a measure of how much "motion" an object has, combining both mass and velocity: p = mv (momentum = mass × velocity). Momentum is a vector quantity — it has both magnitude and direction. Its units are kg·m/s.

A large, slow object can have the same momentum as a small, fast object. A 150 kg Hawaiian canoe moving at 3 m/s has a momentum of 450 kg·m/s — the same as a 5 kg medicine ball thrown at 90 m/s. This equivalence is what makes momentum so useful: it captures the "difficulty of stopping" an object in a single number.

Everyday examples of momentum in action: a heavily loaded cargo ship is difficult to stop (large momentum from large mass); a bullet is dangerous despite its small mass because of its enormous speed (large momentum from high velocity); a slow-rolling boulder on a Hawaiian lava field can cause significant damage due to its large mass.

🌺

Hawaii Connection: During the 2018 Kīlauea eruption, lava "laze" bombs — chunks of hardened lava — were hurled into the air by lava entering the ocean. A 20 kg lava bomb traveling at 15 m/s has momentum of 300 kg·m/s, enough to penetrate boat roofs and cause serious injury. Understanding momentum helps emergency managers calculate safe distances.

Section 2: Conservation of Momentum

One of the most powerful principles in all of physics is the Law of Conservation of Momentum: in a closed system with no external forces, the total momentum before any event (like a collision) equals the total momentum after.

This law is why billiard balls behave predictably, why rockets can propel themselves in space, and why car crash investigators can determine what happened just from the positions of vehicles after a collision.

There are two types of collisions: Elastic collisions, where objects bounce off each other and both momentum AND kinetic energy are conserved (like billiard balls); and Inelastic collisions, where objects stick together after impact and only momentum is conserved (kinetic energy is converted to heat, sound, and deformation).

Example: A 5 kg ball moving at 4 m/s collides with a 3 kg ball at rest. They stick together (perfectly inelastic). Find their speed after collision:
Total momentum before = (5)(4) + (3)(0) = 20 kg·m/s
Total momentum after = (5+3) × v = 8v
8v = 20 → v = 2.5 m/s

✝️

Faith Connection: Conservation laws — momentum, energy, charge — are among the deepest features of physical reality. They reflect the universe's extraordinary consistency: something is always preserved across interactions. The Catholic physicist and priest Georges Lemaître (who first proposed the Big Bang theory) saw such conservation laws as evidence of the universe's created order — a cosmos governed by rational laws given by a rational God.

Section 3: Impulse — Changing Momentum Over Time

Impulse is the product of force and the time over which it acts: J = F × Δt. Impulse equals the change in momentum of an object (J = Δp = m × Δv). This relationship — called the impulse-momentum theorem — has enormous practical importance.

If you must stop an object (change its momentum to zero), the impulse required is fixed (equal to the initial momentum). However, by extending the time of the stopping force, you can dramatically reduce the average force. This is the physics of every safety device humans have invented: helmets, airbags, crash barriers, and padded gym floors all work by extending the time of collision, reducing the force on the person.

A person jumping off a wall and landing on concrete stops in about 0.05 seconds — experiencing a large force. Landing on a sand beach and rolling takes 0.5 seconds — ten times less force for the same change in momentum. This is why Hawaiian surfers practice falling safely: extending the time of impact reduces injury.

Section 4: Rotational Motion and Torque

Rotational motion occurs when an object spins or rotates around an axis. Unlike linear motion (movement along a path), rotational motion is described using concepts like angular velocity (how fast it spins), angular acceleration (how quickly the spin rate changes), and torque.

Torque is the rotational equivalent of force — it is what causes rotation to start, stop, or change. Torque depends on both the force applied AND the distance from the axis of rotation (the lever arm): τ = F × d. A door is easier to open when you push far from the hinges (large lever arm) than near the hinges (small lever arm).

Angular momentum (L) is the rotational equivalent of linear momentum: L = I × ω, where I is the moment of inertia (how mass is distributed around the axis) and ω (omega) is angular velocity. Like linear momentum, angular momentum is conserved when no external torque acts on the system.

🌺

Hawaii Connection: Traditional Hawaiian culture included skilled use of rotational mechanics. The pōhaku (stone sling) spun a stone in a circle before release — building angular momentum that transferred to linear momentum upon release. Hawaiian spinning tops (hue kā) demonstrated conservation of angular momentum. Today, the Mauna Kea Observatories use precision rotating telescope mechanisms to track stars as Earth rotates beneath them.

Section 5: Centripetal Force and Circular Motion

Any object moving in a circle requires a force directed toward the center of the circle — this is called centripetal force (from Latin: "center-seeking"). Centripetal force is not a new type of force; it is simply whatever force happens to point toward the center: gravity (for planets orbiting the Sun), tension (for a stone being swung in a sling), friction (for a car turning a corner), or normal force (for a rollercoaster loop).

The magnitude of centripetal force required is: Fc = mv²/r, where m is mass, v is speed, and r is the radius of the circular path. Greater speed or smaller radius requires greater centripetal force.

A common misconception is "centrifugal force" — the feeling of being thrown outward in a turning car. This is not a real force; it is simply inertia. Your body tends to continue in a straight line (Newton's First Law) while the car turns, so you feel pushed against the door. No force is actually pushing you outward.

Section 6: Rockets and Space Travel

Rockets are perhaps the most dramatic application of Newton's Third Law and conservation of momentum. A rocket engine burns fuel and expels exhaust gases backward at high speed. By Newton's Third Law, the exhaust pushes the rocket forward. By conservation of momentum, the momentum of the exhaust gases backward equals the momentum gained by the rocket forward.

Rockets work in the vacuum of space because they carry their own fuel AND oxidizer (the oxygen needed for combustion). Unlike jet engines (which need atmospheric oxygen), rocket engines are self-contained systems — Newton's Third Law requires nothing to push against except the expelled gas itself.

🌺

Hawaii Connection: The Mauna Kea Science Reserve on Hawaiʻi island hosts 13 telescopes from 11 countries and is one of the premier astronomical observatories on Earth — selected for its high altitude (4,205 m), low humidity, and stable atmosphere above the clouds. These telescopes observe planets, stars, black holes, and distant galaxies — objects that move according to the same gravitational and momentum laws studied in this unit. NASA's Deep Space Network also uses Hawaiian ground stations to communicate with distant spacecraft.

🙏 Faith & Science Reflection

The Hōkūleʻa, the traditional Hawaiian double-hulled voyaging canoe, completed a Mālama Honua ("Care for the Earth") World Voyage in 2017, sailing 60,000 miles to 150+ ports across the Pacific, Atlantic, and Indian Oceans without GPS — guided by stars, waves, and traditional knowledge. This beautiful statement captures the spirit of Laudato Si: the physics of celestial mechanics, momentum, and navigation are not dry facts — they are paths into the wonder of a creation designed with breathtaking intelligence and beauty. Understanding how the universe moves leads us naturally to ask: who set it in motion?

Unit 4

Energy

HCPS III: SC.8.8.7  |  NGSS: HS-PS3-1 to HS-PS3-3  |  ⏱ 3 Weeks
Energy is perhaps the most fundamental concept in all of physics. Everything that happens in the universe involves energy — its storage, transfer, or transformation. When lava erupts from Kīlauea, when a surfer rides a wave, when the sun warms Hawaii's shores, when you eat breakfast before school — all of these involve energy. In this unit, we will explore the different forms of energy, the Law of Conservation of Energy, and how Hawaiʻi is becoming a model for sustainable energy use. As Laudato Si reminds us, caring for energy means caring for our "common home."

Section 1: What Is Energy?

Energy is defined in physics as the ability to do work — to exert a force over a distance, causing motion or change. Energy is measured in Joules (J), named after the English physicist James Prescott Joule. One Joule equals the energy needed to apply a force of 1 Newton over a distance of 1 meter (1 J = 1 N·m).

Energy exists in many forms, but all can be broadly categorized as kinetic energy (energy of motion) or potential energy (stored energy available to do work). The sun's energy powers almost all life on Earth through a cascade of transformations: nuclear fusion in the sun → radiant energy → photosynthesis → chemical energy in plants → food chains → kinetic energy in animals.

Hawaii is unique in having access to almost every form of renewable energy: abundant sunlight, strong trade winds, volcanic geothermal heat, and vast ocean thermal energy. Hawaii has set a goal of 100% renewable electricity by 2045 — an ambitious model for island communities worldwide.

🌺

Hawaii Connection: The Puna Geothermal Venture on Hawaiʻi Island taps volcanic heat to generate electricity, providing about 25% of the Big Island's power. Solar panels generate electricity from Hawaii's approximately 278 sunny days per year. The Kaheawa Wind Farm on Maui harnesses the trade winds. Hawaii's energy transition is one of the most ambitious in the world.

Section 2: Kinetic and Potential Energy

Kinetic Energy (KE) is the energy an object has because of its motion. The formula is: KE = ½mv². Kinetic energy depends on both mass and velocity, but velocity has a greater effect because it is squared — doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy. This is why speed limits on roads matter so much: a car going 60 mph in a crash has four times the kinetic energy of one going 30 mph.

Gravitational Potential Energy (PE) is the stored energy an object has due to its height above a reference point. The formula is: PE = mgh (mass × gravity × height). A rock perched at the rim of Kīlauea Caldera has enormous potential energy that converts to kinetic energy as it falls (or rolls in lava).

Other forms of potential energy include: elastic potential energy (stored in compressed springs or stretched bungee cords), chemical potential energy (stored in bonds between atoms — food, fuel, batteries), nuclear potential energy (stored in atomic nuclei), and electrical potential energy (stored in charged systems).

Energy Comparison:
A 70 kg cliff diver at Waimea Bay's 15 m cliff: PE = 70 × 9.8 × 15 = 10,290 J
A 0.145 kg baseball thrown at 40 m/s: KE = ½(0.145)(40²) = 116 J
The diver at the top of the cliff has 89× more energy than the baseball!

Section 3: The Law of Conservation of Energy

The most important principle in all of energy science is the Law of Conservation of Energy: Energy cannot be created or destroyed — it can only be transformed from one form to another. The total amount of energy in a closed system is always constant, though its form may change.

A classic demonstration: a pendulum. At the highest point of its swing, a pendulum bob has maximum PE and minimum KE (momentarily stopped). As it swings down, PE converts to KE — speed increases. At the bottom of the swing, PE is zero (lowest point) and KE is maximum (fastest speed). As it swings up the other side, KE converts back to PE. The total (KE + PE) remains constant throughout (ignoring air friction).

In the real world, some energy is always converted to thermal energy (heat) through friction and air resistance — energy is still conserved (not lost), but it becomes less useful for doing work. This is why perpetual motion machines are impossible: every real machine loses some energy to heat.

✝️

Faith Connection: The Law of Conservation of Energy is one of the most fundamental laws of nature. Nothing in the physical universe can create or destroy energy — only transform it. Many theologians note that only God, as the ultimate ground of being, can create from nothing (creatio ex nihilo). The conservation law applies within creation; creation itself, however, originates in God's creative act. "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth" (Genesis 1:1) — this creation was the original input of energy and matter into the universe.

Section 4: Work and Power

Work in physics has a specific meaning: it is done when a force moves an object in the direction of that force. The formula is: W = F × d (force times displacement in the direction of force), measured in Joules.

Importantly, in physics you can work very hard and do zero work if there is no displacement in the direction of force. A student who holds a heavy textbook above their head is exerting a force (upward) and getting tired — but doing no work (because the book isn't moving, so d = 0). Similarly, carrying a backpack horizontally requires a force (upward against gravity) perpendicular to the motion (horizontal) — physics says no work is done on the backpack.

Power is the rate at which work is done: P = W / t (work divided by time), measured in Watts (W). One watt equals one joule per second. A 100 W light bulb uses 100 J of electrical energy every second. The average human body at rest uses about 80 W of metabolic power; a hard-paddling outrigger canoe racer might produce 400–500 W.

Section 5: Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy

Efficiency measures how well a machine converts input energy to useful output energy: Efficiency (%) = (Useful output ÷ Total input) × 100%. No machine is 100% efficient — some energy is always lost as heat due to friction and other processes. A modern electric car is about 77–90% efficient (electrical to mechanical); a gasoline engine is only about 20–40% efficient.

Renewable energy comes from naturally replenishing sources: solar, wind, water (hydro), geothermal, and biomass. Nonrenewable energy comes from fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) that took millions of years to form and are being consumed far faster than they can be replaced.

Hawaii imports about 90% of its energy in the form of petroleum — a significant economic and environmental burden. The state's renewable energy goals aim to reduce this dependence by maximizing the use of Hawaii's natural energy resources: volcanic geothermal heat, abundant sunshine, consistent trade winds, and the vast thermal energy stored in the ocean.

🌺

Hawaii Connection: The Ocean Thermal Energy Conversion (OTEC) technology was pioneered in Hawaii. OTEC uses the temperature difference between warm surface water (about 25°C) and cold deep water (about 5°C at 1,000 m depth) to generate electricity. Hawaii's Marine Center in Kailua-Kona has operated OTEC demonstration plants. This technology could provide continuous, renewable electricity for Hawaii without any intermittency (unlike solar and wind).

Waves, Sound & Light

HCPS III: SC.8.8.8–SC.8.8.10  |  NGSS: HS-PS4-1 to HS-PS4-5  |  ⏱ 6 Weeks
The universe communicates through waves. Light from distant stars, sound from Hawaiian music, radio signals carrying news, the ocean swells that guided traditional Polynesian navigators — all are forms of wave energy traveling through space and matter. Unit 5 brings together the physics of waves, sound, and light in a rich exploration of how energy moves through the world. From the Pacific Tsunami Warning Center in Honolulu to the rainbows that arch over Manoa Valley, waves and light reveal the extraordinary beauty and order of God's creation.

Section 1: Properties of Waves

A wave is a disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transferring matter. Waves move energy through a medium (like water, air, or solid rock) or through a vacuum (as electromagnetic waves like light can do).

All waves share the same fundamental properties:

  • Amplitude: The maximum displacement of the medium from its rest (equilibrium) position. Greater amplitude means more energy in the wave. For ocean waves, amplitude relates to wave height — the distance from rest to crest.
  • Wavelength (λ): The distance of one complete wave cycle, measured from crest to crest (or trough to trough). Symbol: λ (lambda). Units: meters.
  • Frequency (f): The number of complete wave cycles that pass a fixed point per second. Units: Hertz (Hz = cycles/second).
  • Period (T): The time for one complete wave cycle to pass a point: T = 1/f. Units: seconds.
  • Wave speed (v): How fast the wave moves through the medium: v = f × λ. Units: m/s.
🌺

Hawaii Connection: Hawaiian surfers and traditional navigators are experts at reading wave properties. The north shore of Oʻahu (Waimea Bay, Pipeline, Sunset Beach) receives giant swells generated by storms in the North Pacific, thousands of miles away. These waves travel across open ocean at speeds of 40–90 km/h, with wavelengths of 100–300 meters and periods of 12–20 seconds. Expert surfers can identify a coming swell set by changes in period and direction days before it arrives.

Section 2: Types of Waves — Transverse and Longitudinal

Waves are classified by the direction of particle vibration relative to the direction of wave travel.

In a transverse wave, the medium's particles vibrate perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction the wave travels. If you shake one end of a rope up and down while the wave travels horizontally, the rope particles move vertically — that's transverse. Ocean surface waves, waves on a guitar string, and all electromagnetic waves (including light) are transverse waves.

In a longitudinal wave, the medium's particles vibrate parallel (in the same direction) to the direction the wave travels. A slinky pushed and pulled along its length demonstrates longitudinal motion: the coils compress (compressions) and spread apart (rarefactions) as the wave passes. Sound waves are longitudinal — they create alternating compressions and rarefactions in the air (or water or solid material).

Sound requires a medium to travel through — it cannot travel through a vacuum (which is why space is silent). Sound travels fastest through solids (about 6,000 m/s in steel), slower through liquids (about 1,500 m/s in water), and slowest through gases (about 343 m/s in air at room temperature).

✝️

Faith Connection: Traditional Hawaiian music — the oli (chant), hula, and the resonant sounds of the ipu (gourd drum) and pahu (shark-skin drum) — create longitudinal sound waves that carry culture, prayer, and meaning across generations. Catholic liturgical music similarly uses sound waves to lift hearts and minds toward God. The beauty of music — whether a sunrise oli on Mauna Kea or a Gregorian chant in a cathedral — is physics and spirituality intertwined.

Section 3: Sound — The Doppler Effect and Musical Resonance

The Doppler Effect describes the change in observed frequency (and pitch for sound) when a wave source and an observer are moving relative to each other. When a source moves toward you, the sound waves ahead of it are compressed — you hear a higher pitch. When the source moves away, the waves are stretched — you hear a lower pitch. The familiar "EEEEE-owww" of a race car passing is a classic Doppler demonstration.

The Doppler Effect applies to all waves, including light. When distant galaxies move away from us (which most are), their light is stretched to longer, redder wavelengths — this is called redshift. Astronomers use redshift to calculate how fast galaxies are moving away from us, which is key evidence that the universe has been expanding since the Big Bang.

Resonance occurs when an object vibrates at its natural frequency in response to an external vibration at the same frequency, producing dramatic amplification. Musical instruments are built around resonance: a ukulele string vibrates at specific frequencies, and the hollow wooden body resonates at those frequencies, amplifying the sound. The beautiful acoustics of Kawaiahao Church in Honolulu result from resonance within the coral stone walls.

Section 4: Electromagnetic Waves and the Electromagnetic Spectrum

Unlike sound waves, electromagnetic (EM) waves do not require a medium — they can travel through a vacuum at the speed of light (approximately 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s). All EM waves are transverse waves consisting of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and to the direction of travel.

Section 5: Light — Reflection, Refraction, and Color

Reflection occurs when a wave bounces off a surface. The Law of Reflection states: the angle at which light strikes a surface (angle of incidence) equals the angle at which it bounces off (angle of reflection). Both angles are measured from the normal — an imaginary line perpendicular to the surface at the point of contact.

Refraction is the bending of a wave when it crosses the boundary between two different media (such as air and water) where it travels at different speeds. Light slows down when entering a denser medium (like glass or water) and bends toward the normal. This is why a straw in a glass of water appears broken at the water's surface, and why Hawaiian reef fish appear to be in a different position than they actually are — an important lesson for traditional Hawaiian spearfishers.

White light is a mixture of all colors of the visible spectrum. When light passes through a prism or water droplets, different wavelengths refract by different amounts, separating white light into the familiar rainbow spectrum (ROYGBIV: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet). The frequent rainbows in Mānoa Valley and over the Ko'olau Mountains result from sunlight refracting through water droplets in the air after rain.

Diffraction occurs when waves bend around obstacles or spread through openings. The smaller the opening relative to the wavelength, the greater the diffraction. Traditional Polynesian navigators knew that ocean swells "bend" around islands, and by feeling the diffracted swells, they could detect the presence of land even before it was visible.

Section 6: Tsunamis — Waves of Destruction and Warning

A tsunami is a series of large ocean waves generated by sudden seafloor displacement — typically from underwater earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or submarine landslides. Despite their devastating effects near shore, tsunamis in the open ocean are deceptively subtle: they may have wavelengths of 500+ km, travel at 800 km/h, but have amplitudes of only 0.5–1 m in deep water — unnoticeable to ships at sea.

As a tsunami enters shallow water near the coast, its speed decreases (wave speed depends on depth), but its amplitude (height) increases dramatically to 10–30 meters or more — a process called wave shoaling. This is why "tidal waves" can appear suddenly and with catastrophic force.

The Pacific Tsunami Warning Center (PTWC), headquartered in Honolulu on Oʻahu, monitors seismic activity and ocean wave sensors across the Pacific 24/7. In 1946, a tsunami generated by an earthquake near Alaska killed 159 people in Hawaii, including 16 schoolchildren in Hilo who ran toward the receding water (not understanding it was the wave drawing back before striking). Hawaii now has extensive tsunami education, evacuation routes, and early warning systems — a community response to physics.

🌺

Hawaii Connection: Traditional Hawaiian culture preserved knowledge of tsunamis (referred to in oral histories as "the waves that swallowed the land"). Communities built on higher ground and recognized warning signs — rapid sea withdrawal, strange sounds, unusual animal behavior — that modern scientists now understand as wave physics. Indigenous knowledge and modern physics both point to the same truth: ocean waves carry enormous energy that demands respect.

Unit 6: Waves — v = fλ

⏱ 3 Weeks 📋 NGSS: MS-PS4-1 | HCPS III: SC.8.5.1

Sound, light, radio, X-rays, tsunamis — all are waves. Understanding waves through the equation v = fλ unlocks everything from sonar depth-finding to the design of concert halls to how your cell phone communicates. Waves are the carriers of information and energy across our universe.

〰️ Wave Properties

A wave is a disturbance that transfers energy through matter or space without permanently moving the medium. Key properties:

  • Wavelength (λ, lambda): Distance between two consecutive identical points (crest to crest). Unit: meters (m)
  • Frequency (f): Number of complete wave cycles per second. Unit: hertz (Hz) = cycles/second
  • Wave speed (v): How fast the wave travels through a medium. Unit: m/s
  • Amplitude: Maximum displacement from rest position (relates to energy, not speed)

The Wave Equation: v = f × λ
Speed = Frequency × Wavelength
Rearranged: f = v/λ  |  λ = v/f

SONAR (Sound Navigation And Ranging) uses sound waves to map the ocean floor. A pulse of sound is sent downward; the time until the echo returns is measured:

Divide by 2 because sound travels to the bottom AND back.

Unit 7: Electricity & Circuits

⏱ 3 Weeks 📋 NGSS: MS-PS3-2 | HCPS III: SC.8.5.3

Every time you turn on a light switch, charge a phone, or run a fan, you are applying the same equations engineers use to design Hawaii's solar grid and power grid. Ohm's Law and circuit analysis are the foundation of all electrical technology.

⚡ Ohm's Law: V = IR

Three fundamental quantities describe any electrical circuit:

  • Voltage (V) — electrical "pressure" that pushes current; measured in Volts (V)
  • Current (I) — flow of electric charge; measured in Amperes/Amps (A)
  • Resistance (R) — opposition to current flow; measured in Ohms (Ω)

Ohm's Law: V = I × R
Rearranged: I = V/R  |  R = V/I
Analogy: Voltage = water pressure, Current = water flow rate, Resistance = pipe narrowness

Example: A 12V battery drives current through a 4Ω resistor. Current = V/R = 12/4 = 3 Amps

💡 Power: P = IV

Electrical power (P) is the rate of energy use or delivery. Measured in Watts (W) = Joules/second:

P = I × V = I²R = V²/R
Example: A 3A current through a 12V source: P = 3 × 12 = 36 Watts

Energy used over time: Energy = P × t (in Watts × seconds = Joules, or kWh for electricity bills)
1 kWh = a 1,000W device running for 1 hour = 3,600,000 J

🔌 Series and Parallel Circuits

Series circuit: Components connected end-to-end; same current flows through all:

Total resistance: R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
Example: 3Ω + 5Ω + 2Ω = 10Ω total
Same current through each; voltage splits across resistors

Parallel circuit: Components connected side-by-side; same voltage across each:

Total resistance: 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃
Example: 1/6 + 1/3 = 1/6 + 2/6 = 3/6 → R_total = 2Ω
Total resistance is LESS than any individual — more paths = less opposition

Unit 8: Optics & Snell's Law

⏱ 3 Weeks 📋 NGSS: MS-PS4-2 | HCPS III: SC.8.5.2

Why does a straw look bent in a glass of water? Why do diamonds sparkle so brilliantly? Why can fiber optic cables carry internet signals for thousands of miles with almost no loss? The answer to all three is Snell's Law — the mathematics of light bending at boundaries between materials.

🔭 Reflection and Refraction

Reflection: When light bounces off a surface. Law of reflection: the angle of incidence (θ_i) equals the angle of reflection (θ_r) — both measured from the normal (perpendicular to the surface).

Refraction: When light bends as it passes from one transparent material to another, because its speed changes. Light slows down when entering a denser medium, causing it to bend toward the normal. Light speeds up when entering a less dense medium, bending away from the normal.

The index of refraction (n) measures how much a material slows light compared to its speed in vacuum:

n = c / v (c = speed of light in vacuum = 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s; v = speed of light in material)
n = 1.0 (vacuum/air)  |  n = 1.33 (water)  |  n = 1.5 (glass)  |  n = 2.42 (diamond)

📐 Snell's Law

n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂
n₁ = index of refraction of medium 1   θ₁ = angle of incidence
n₂ = index of refraction of medium 2   θ₂ = angle of refraction

Example: Light passes from water (n=1.33) into glass (n=1.5) at θ₁=30°.
1.33 × sin(30°) = 1.5 × sin θ₂
1.33 × 0.5 = 1.5 × sin θ₂
0.665 = 1.5 × sin θ₂
sin θ₂ = 0.665/1.5 = 0.443
θ₂ = arcsin(0.443) ≈ 26.3° (smaller angle — bent toward normal, as expected going into denser medium)

💎 Total Internal Reflection: Diamonds and Fiber Optics

Total internal reflection (TIR) occurs when light tries to pass from a DENSER medium to a LESS dense medium at an angle GREATER than the critical angle (θ_c). Instead of refracting, all light reflects internally.

Critical angle formula: sin θ_c = n₂/n₁ (n₂ < n₁)
For diamond-air: sin θ_c = 1.0/2.42 = 0.413 → θ_c = 24.4°
This tiny critical angle means light bouncing around inside a diamond is almost always totally internally reflected — creating the brilliant sparkle!

🌊

Hawaii's Fiber Optic Internet: Hawaii is connected to the mainland US and Asia by undersea fiber optic cables running thousands of kilometers across the Pacific Ocean floor. Light signals travel through the glass fiber core and are kept inside by total internal reflection — bouncing millions of times without escaping. This is how you can video-call the mainland in real time.

Unit 9: Kepler's Laws & Orbital Mechanics

⏱ 3 Weeks 📋 NGSS: MS-ESS1-2 | HCPS III: SC.8.6.1

How do scientists know how long it takes a distant planet to orbit its star? How are we finding potentially habitable exoplanets light-years away? The answer was published in 1619 by Johannes Kepler — and it's still the foundation of planetary science today, now extended to thousands of worlds beyond our Solar System.

🪐 Kepler's Three Laws

Kepler's First Law (Elliptical Orbits): Every planet orbits the Sun in an ellipse, with the Sun at one focus. Orbits are not perfect circles — Earth's orbit is slightly elliptical (closer to the Sun in January, farther in July).

Kepler's Second Law (Equal Areas): A line connecting a planet to the Sun sweeps equal areas in equal time. Consequence: planets move FASTER when closer to the Sun and SLOWER when farther away.

Kepler's Third Law (T² = r³): The square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of its average distance from the Sun.

T² = r³
T = orbital period in Earth years   r = average orbital radius in Astronomical Units (AU)
1 AU = Earth-Sun distance = 1.496 × 10¹¹ m ≈ 150 million km

📐 Calculating Orbital Periods and Distances

Example 1: Mars is 1.524 AU from the Sun. What is its orbital period?
T² = r³ = (1.524)³ = 3.540
T = √3.540 = 1.881 years (actual: 1.881 years ✓)

Example 2: A planet has a period of 8 years. How far is it from the Sun?
T² = r³ → 8² = r³ → 64 = r³ → r = ∛64 = 4 AU

🌍 Exoplanet Habitable Zones

NASA uses Kepler's Third Law to characterize exoplanets (planets orbiting other stars). For a sun-like star, the habitable zone (where liquid water can exist) is roughly 0.95–1.37 AU. Kepler's Law allows scientists to calculate where a planet orbits from the TIMING of its transits (how regularly it crosses in front of its star).

As of 2024, over 5,600 confirmed exoplanets have been discovered. About 60 are in confirmed habitable zones. The search continues using telescopes like JWST.

⭐

Hawaii's Role in Exoplanet Discovery: The W.M. Keck Observatory on Mauna Kea has confirmed hundreds of exoplanets using radial velocity measurements. Mauna Kea's altitude (4,205 m), dry atmosphere, and dark skies make it one of Earth's premier astronomical sites. Hawaii literally sits where Earth science meets space science.

✝️ Faith Reflection: Wonder at the Cosmos

Kepler discovered his three laws while working as an imperial mathematician — but he wrote extensively about the universe as a reflection of God's mathematical mind. He called his astronomy "thinking God's thoughts after him." The precision of T² = r³, holding perfectly across every planet and exoplanet, suggests an order embedded in creation itself.

The possibility of other habitable worlds raises profound theological questions the Church engages with seriously. The Vatican Observatory's Director, Br. Guy Consolmagno, SJ, writes: "The universe is so vast and so magnificent that there's no reason to think that God should limit God's self to just us." Astronomy deepens wonder — the appropriate response to God's creation is awe, inquiry, and humility.

In Hawaiian tradition, the stars (nā hōkū) are not merely navigation tools — they are ancestors and divine signs. Mauna Kea, the mountain where astronomers look for new worlds, is itself the most sacred mountain in Hawaii. Science and the sacred coexist at the summit of Mauna Kea in a tension that calls us all to respect.
