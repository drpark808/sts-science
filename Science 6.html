🌍 What Is Earth Science?

Earth Science is the study of our planet — its structure, history, atmosphere, oceans, and place in the universe. Earth scientists ask questions like: Why does the ground shake? How do mountains form? What causes seasons? Why is Hawaii's weather so different on each side of an island?

Earth Science has four major branches:

  • Geology — the study of Earth's structure, rocks, minerals, and processes (volcanoes, earthquakes, plate tectonics)
  • Meteorology — the study of weather and the atmosphere
  • Oceanography — the study of Earth's oceans, currents, tides, and marine features
  • Astronomy — the study of Earth's place in space (seasons, Moon phases, solar system)

🧪 The Scientific Method

1
Observation
Notice something in the natural world. Use senses and instruments. Example: "The rocks on the windward coast of Oahu are much rounder than rocks on the leeward side."
2
Question
Ask a specific, testable question. Example: "Does wave action cause rocks to become rounder over time?"
3
Hypothesis
IF-THEN prediction: "IF rocks are tumbled in water for longer periods, THEN they will become rounder and smoother."
4
Experiment
Test ONE variable at a time. Control all others. Design a fair test.
5
Data Analysis
Organize results in tables and graphs. Look for patterns and trends in your data.
6
Conclusion
Was the hypothesis supported? What did you learn? What new questions arise?

The scientific method is a cycle — every conclusion raises new questions. Science is never finished.

📊 Variables and Experimental Design

  • Independent Variable (IV): What the scientist changes (the "cause")
  • Dependent Variable (DV): What the scientist measures (the "effect")
  • Controlled Variables: Everything kept the same to make the test fair
  • Control Group: No experimental treatment — the baseline for comparison

Earth Science Example: Testing whether soil type affects erosion rate:
• IV: Type of soil (sandy, clay, loam)
• DV: Amount of soil eroded by water (grams)
• Controlled: Amount of water, slope angle, duration, container size

A control group provides a baseline for comparison. Without it, scientists cannot tell whether the results were caused by the experimental treatment or by other factors.

Unit 2: Earth's Structure, Plate Tectonics, Rocks & Minerals

⏱ 8 Weeks 📋 HCPS III: SC.6.6.2–SC.6.6.3 | NGSS: MS-ESS2-1–MS-ESS2-3

Beneath your feet, right now, is one of the most dynamic systems on Earth. The ground feels solid and still — but Earth's interior is churning with heat, and the surface is broken into massive plates that slowly move, collide, and pull apart, building mountains, triggering earthquakes, and fueling volcanoes. Nowhere is this more visible than in Hawaiʻi.

🌐 Earth's Layers

Earth is structured in concentric layers, like an onion, each with different composition and properties:

LayerStateCompositionThickness
CrustSolidOceanic: basalt; Continental: granite5–70 km
MantleSemi-solid (plastic)Iron/magnesium silicate rock~2,900 km
Outer CoreLiquidIron and nickel~2,300 km

Earth's interior heat comes from two sources: residual heat from Earth's formation 4.5 billion years ago, and heat from radioactive decay of elements in the mantle and crust. This heat drives convection currents in the mantle — the engine behind plate tectonics.

🗺️ Plate Tectonics

Earth's crust and upper mantle are broken into about 15–20 major tectonic plates that float and move on the plastic mantle below. They move at 2–15 cm per year — about as fast as your fingernails grow. The three types of plate boundaries:

Boundary TypeWhat HappensFeatures CreatedExample
ConvergentPlates push toward each otherMountains, ocean trenches, volcanoesHimalayas (India + Eurasia); Cascades
DivergentPlates pull apartRift valleys, mid-ocean ridges, new crustMid-Atlantic Ridge; East African Rift
TransformPlates slide past each otherFault lines, earthquakes (no volcanoes)San Andreas Fault, California
🌋

Hawaiʻi Hot Spot: Hawaii is NOT at a plate boundary — it sits in the middle of the Pacific Plate over a hot spot: a fixed plume of superheated mantle magma. As the Pacific Plate moves northwest at ~7 cm/year, new volcanoes form over the hot spot. The Big Island is the youngest and most active. Kauaʻi, the oldest main island (~5 million years), is farthest northwest. A new seamount, Lōʻihi, is forming underwater southeast of the Big Island — the next Hawaiian island!

🌋 Volcanoes and Earthquakes

Hawaiian volcanoes are shield volcanoes — wide, gently sloping mountains built from low-viscosity basaltic lava that flows long distances. They are less explosive than composite (stratovolcanoes) but produce enormous volumes of lava over time.

Hawaiian lava types: Pāhoehoe (smooth, ropy lava — lower silica, slower flow) and ʻAʻā (rough, jagged clinker lava — faster flow, more degassing). Both are basalt.

Earthquakes occur when stress built up along fault lines is suddenly released as seismic waves. The Richter scale measures earthquake magnitude (energy released); each whole number = 10× more ground motion. The point underground where an earthquake originates is the focus; the point directly above on the surface is the epicenter.

🪨 The Rock Cycle

All rocks are continuously transformed from one type to another through the rock cycle — driven by heat from Earth's interior and energy from the Sun:

  • Igneous rock forms when magma or lava cools and solidifies. Intrusive (underground, slow cooling) = large crystals (granite). Extrusive (at surface, fast cooling) = small crystals or glassy texture (basalt, obsidian).
  • Sedimentary rock forms when weathered rock fragments (sediment) are deposited, compacted, and cemented. Types: clastic (sandstone, shale), chemical (rock salt, limestone), organic (coal, chalk).
  • Metamorphic rock forms when existing rocks are changed by intense heat and/or pressure without melting. Examples: marble (from limestone), quartzite (from sandstone), schist (from shale).

Rocks can cycle between types: igneous rock weathers into sediment → sedimentary rock → buried and heated → metamorphic rock → melted → magma → igneous rock again.

💨 Weathering, Erosion, and Soil

Weathering breaks rocks into smaller pieces. Physical weathering: breaks rock without changing its chemistry (freeze-thaw cycles, wave action, root wedging). Chemical weathering: changes rock's chemical composition (acid rain, oxidation — rust, carbonation).

Erosion transports weathered material by water, wind, ice, or gravity. Deposition occurs when the agent of erosion loses energy and drops its sediment. Hawaii's beautiful beaches are formed by deposition of eroded volcanic rock and coral fragments.

Soil forms from weathered rock (parent material) plus organic matter (humus). Soil has distinct layers called horizons: O (organic litter) → A (topsoil, rich in humus) → B (subsoil) → C (weathered parent rock) → R (bedrock).

✝️ Faith Reflection: Sacred Land and Stewardship

In Hawaiian tradition, the volcanic landscape is not just geology — it is deeply sacred. Pele (Pelehonuamea), the goddess of volcanoes and fire, is revered as the creator of the Hawaiian Islands. When lava flows across the land, Hawaiians see Pele at work — building, destroying, and renewing. The land itself is considered a living ancestor (ʻāina = that which feeds).

The Catholic tradition also teaches that Earth is not merely a resource to be exploited — it is creation, a gift from God, entrusted to our care. Pope Francis writes in Laudato Si: "The land belongs to God. We are only stewards."

Both traditions share a conviction: the Earth is sacred, and we are responsible for its wellbeing. Studying plate tectonics, volcanoes, and the rock cycle deepens our awe for the planet's power and complexity — and our responsibility to protect it.

Question 7
Explain the processes of WEATHERING, EROSION, and DEPOSITION. How do these processes shape Hawaii's landscape? Give specific Hawaiian examples.
  • Weathering = breakdown of rock in place. Physical: wave action rounds lava rocks on Hawaii's shores; freeze-thaw cracks rock at high elevations on Mauna Kea. At a convergent boundary where an oceanic plate meets a continental plate, the oceanic plate usually:

Unit 3: Weather and Climate

⏱ 8 Weeks 📋 HCPS III: SC.6.6.4–SC.6.6.5 | NGSS: MS-ESS2-5–MS-ESS2-6

Step outside in Hawaii and you experience Earth's atmosphere in action — warm trade winds from the northeast, towering cumulus clouds over the Ko'olau mountains, a sudden rainbow after a passing shower, or the rare Kona storm that brings heavy rain from the south. Weather shapes how we live, and climate shapes everything from ecosystems to agriculture. In this unit, we explore how Earth's atmosphere works.

🌫️ Earth's Atmosphere

Earth's atmosphere is a thin blanket of gases held by gravity. It contains ~78% nitrogen (N₂), ~21% oxygen (O₂), ~0.9% argon, ~0.04% carbon dioxide (CO₂), and trace amounts of other gases. It is divided into layers:

LayerAltitudeKey Features
Troposphere0–12 kmWhere all weather occurs; temperature decreases with altitude
Stratosphere12–50 kmContains the ozone layer (O₃) that absorbs UV radiation; temperature increases with altitude
Mesosphere50–80 kmMeteors burn up here; coldest layer (−90°C)
Thermosphere80–600 kmAurora borealis/australis; very thin air; ISS orbits here; temperatures can reach 2,000°C (but very little matter)
Exosphere600+ kmTransition to outer space; satellites orbit here

🌦️ Weather vs. Climate

Weather is the short-term state of the atmosphere at a specific time and place (temperature, precipitation, wind, humidity). Climate is the long-term average of weather patterns in a region over at least 30 years.

"Climate is what you expect; weather is what you get."

🌺

Hawaiʻi Microclimates: Hawaii has some of the most dramatic microclimates on Earth. Mount Waiʻaleʻale on Kauaʻi receives ~450 inches (11,400 mm) of rain per year — one of Earth's wettest spots. Yet just 15 miles away on the leeward coast, annual rainfall is only ~20 inches. The cause: the rain shadow effect — northeast trade winds carry moisture-laden air that rises over mountain ranges, cools, condenses, and drops rain on the windward side. The leeward (southwest) side is left dry.

☁️ Clouds and Precipitation

Clouds form when moist air rises, cools, and water vapor condenses around tiny particles (dust, pollen, sea salt). Cloud types are classified by altitude and shape:

  • Cirrus — High altitude (>6 km), thin, wispy ice crystals; signal fair weather or approaching front
  • Cumulus — Middle altitude, puffy, flat-bottomed; fair weather (small) or developing storms (large)
  • Stratus — Low, flat, gray sheets covering the sky; associated with drizzle or overcast skies
  • Cumulonimbus (Cb) — Massive storm clouds reaching the tropopause; produce thunderstorms, heavy rain, lightning, hail, and tornadoes
  • Nimbostratus — Low, dark, thick layer; produces steady moderate rain or snow

Precipitation forms when cloud droplets combine until heavy enough to fall as rain, snow, sleet, or hail.

⛈️ Severe Weather and Hurricanes

Hawaii's most significant severe weather threat is hurricanes (tropical cyclones in the Central/Eastern Pacific). Hurricanes are massive rotating storms powered by warm ocean water (≥26°C/79°F). As warm, moist air rises over the warm ocean, it creates low pressure at the surface, more air rushes in, and Earth's rotation (Coriolis effect) causes the storm to spin counterclockwise in the Northern Hemisphere.

Notable Hawaii hurricanes: Hurricane Iniki (1992) devastated Kauaʻi with winds of 145 mph — the strongest hurricane to ever hit Hawaii. Hawaii's hurricane season peaks August–October when ocean temperatures are highest.

Other severe weather affecting Hawaii: Kona storms (winter storms from the south bringing heavy rain, high surf, and flooding), flash floods (common on steep volcanic terrain), and flash droughts on leeward coasts.

🌡️ Climate Change

Earth's climate is warming due to increased greenhouse gases from human activities. The greenhouse effect: solar radiation passes through the atmosphere and warms Earth's surface; Earth radiates heat (infrared) back upward; greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, water vapor) absorb and re-emit this heat, keeping Earth warm. Without any greenhouse effect, Earth would be −18°C — too cold for life.

The problem: human burning of fossil fuels has increased atmospheric CO₂ from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial) to over 420 ppm today — the highest in 3 million years. This enhanced greenhouse effect is warming the planet ~1.1°C above pre-industrial average.

Effects on Hawaii: Coral bleaching from warming oceans; rising sea levels threatening coastal communities; increased hurricane intensity; shifting rainfall patterns; loss of high-elevation habitat for native birds.

✝️ Faith Reflection: Caring for Our Atmosphere

Pope Francis's Laudato Si (2015) dedicates an entire chapter to climate change: "Climate change is a global problem with grave implications: environmental, social, economic, political and for the distribution of goods. It represents one of the principal challenges facing humanity in our day."

The people most affected by climate change are the world's poorest — communities on low-lying Pacific islands, subsistence farmers, and coastal communities. For Catholics, this connects climate science to social justice: caring for the Earth is also caring for the poor and vulnerable. This is what Pope Francis means by hearing "the cry of the Earth and the cry of the poor."

Hawaii — surrounded by rising seas and warming oceans — is on the front lines. As stewards of these islands, we are called to understand climate science and act.

Unit 4: Earth's Systems, Interactions & Ocean Science

⏱ 8 Weeks 📋 HCPS III: SC.6.6.1, SC.6.6.6 | NGSS: MS-ESS2-4, MS-ESS2-6, MS-ESS3-1

Earth is a water planet. Seen from space, our world is a brilliant blue sphere — 71% covered by ocean. Water moves constantly between ocean, atmosphere, land, and living things in a cycle that has been running for billions of years. And for the Polynesian peoples of the Pacific, understanding water — ocean currents, stars, swells, and winds — was a matter of life and survival.

🌐 Earth's Four Spheres

Earth functions as a system of four interconnected spheres that constantly exchange matter and energy:

SphereDefinitionHawaiian Examples
GeosphereSolid Earth — rocks, soil, minerals, tectonic platesVolcanic rock, lava flows, soil, Mauna Kea
HydrosphereAll of Earth's water — oceans, rivers, groundwater, ice, water vaporPacific Ocean, Waimea River, artesian wells, rain
AtmosphereLayer of gases surrounding EarthTrade winds, clouds, hurricanes, rain
BiosphereAll living organisms and their environmentsCoral reefs, native forests, marine mammals, people

These spheres interact constantly. Example: a volcanic eruption (geosphere) releases gases (atmosphere) that cool the climate, affecting rain patterns (hydrosphere) that impact native plant growth (biosphere). Everything is connected.

💧 The Water Cycle

The water cycle (hydrologic cycle) describes the continuous movement of water through Earth's systems. Key processes:

  • Evaporation: Solar energy converts liquid water (oceans, lakes) to water vapor
  • Transpiration: Plants release water vapor through their leaves. Evaporation + transpiration = evapotranspiration
  • Condensation: Water vapor cools, forms clouds (liquid droplets or ice crystals around particles)
  • Precipitation: Water falls from clouds as rain, snow, sleet, or hail
  • Surface runoff: Water flows over land into streams and rivers
  • Infiltration: Water soaks into soil and becomes groundwater
  • Groundwater flow: Water moves slowly underground, eventually discharging into the ocean or emerging as springs
🌺

Hawaiʻi Connection: Hawaii's native forests are vital to the water cycle. ʻŌhiʻa lehua and other native trees capture cloud moisture (a process called cloud stripping) and channel it into the groundwater. Without healthy upland forests, Hawaii's freshwater supply is threatened. This is why reforestation is a critical conservation priority.

🌊 Ocean Science

The ocean covers 71% of Earth's surface and averages 3,688 m (12,100 ft) deep. Ocean zones by depth:

ZoneDepthLightLife
Intertidal0 m (shoreline)Full sunlightSea stars, anemones, crabs

Ocean currents move heat around the planet. Surface currents are driven by wind. Deep ocean currents (thermohaline circulation) are driven by differences in water temperature and salinity — cold, salty water sinks and flows along the ocean floor.

Tides are caused by the gravitational pull of the Moon (and Sun). The Moon's gravity creates a bulge of water on the side of Earth facing the Moon and another on the opposite side — producing two high tides and two low tides per day.

Tsunamis are not surface waves but displacement waves caused by sudden vertical movement of the ocean floor (earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, landslides). In open ocean they may be only 1 m high but move at 700–800 km/h. As they approach shore and the seafloor rises, they slow down and grow dramatically. The 1946 Aleutian earthquake tsunami struck Hilo, Hawaii with 14-meter waves, killing 159 people.

⭐ Traditional Hawaiian Navigation

Polynesian peoples made the greatest open-ocean voyages in human history — sailing thousands of miles across the Pacific to settle Hawaii, New Zealand, Easter Island, and beyond — in double-hulled canoes (waʻa kaulua) with no instruments. Their navigation methods included:

  • Star navigation: Memorizing the rising and setting points of ~200 stars across the horizon to maintain direction
  • Ocean swell patterns: Feeling the direction of primary swells (generated by distant storms) by lying in the hull of the canoe
  • Wind patterns: Knowing prevailing trade winds and recognizing seasonal wind shifts
  • Cloud formations: Islands cause distinctive cloud patterns — flat-bottomed clouds form over land even when land is below the horizon
  • Bird behavior: Knowing which migratory birds travel toward land at certain times of day
  • Ocean color and phosphorescence: Detecting shallow water, currents, and land proximity
🚢

Hōkūleʻa: In 1976, Hawaiian navigator Mau Piailug guided the voyaging canoe Hōkūleʻa from Hawaii to Tahiti using traditional navigation — no instruments — reviving this nearly lost art. In 2014–2017, Hōkūleʻa circumnavigated the globe on the Mālama Honua (caring for Earth) voyage, spreading the message of ocean stewardship to 85 countries.

🌊 Hawaiian Water Management

Traditional Hawaiians were master water managers. The ahupuaʻa system managed watersheds from mountain to sea as integrated units. Loko iʻa (fishponds) were sophisticated aquaculture systems — up to 400 in ancient Hawaii — using stone walls and sluice gates to trap and raise fish where freshwater streams met the ocean. The largest, Kealia Pond on Maui, covered 600 acres. Traditional Hawaiian irrigation (ʻauwai) channeled stream water to taro (kalo) fields — Hawaii's most sacred food crop.

✝️ Faith Reflection: Water as Sacred Gift

In Catholic tradition, water is the primary sacramental element — used in Baptism, Holy Water, and blessing. Scripture is filled with water imagery: the waters of creation, the Red Sea crossing, Jesus walking on water, the woman at the well. Water is life.

Pope Francis writes in Laudato Si: "Access to safe drinkable water is a basic and universal human right... Our world has a grave social debt towards the poor who lack access to drinking water."

In Hawaiian tradition, wai (water) is so essential that the word for wealth is waiwai — literally "double water." Mālama ʻāina means caring for the land — and water is the blood of the land. When we protect Hawaii's forests, streams, coral reefs, and aquifers, we protect life itself.

Question 5
Explain what causes TSUNAMIS. How are they different from regular wind-driven waves? Describe what happened in the 1946 Hilo tsunami. What tsunami warning systems protect Hawaii today?
Causes: Tsunamis are caused by sudden vertical displacement of the ocean floor from: (1) undersea earthquakes (most common), (2) underwater volcanic eruptions, (3) submarine landslides, (4) rarely, large meteor impacts.

Difference from wind waves: Wind waves affect only the surface (~100 m deep); tsunamis involve the entire water column from seafloor to surface. In open ocean, tsunamis may be only 0.5–1 m tall but travel at 700–900 km/h and have wavelengths of 200+ km. As they approach shore, they slow down (friction with seafloor) and grow dramatically in height.

1946 Hilo Tsunami: Triggered by a magnitude 8.6 earthquake in the Aleutian Islands, Alaska. Waves struck Hilo 4.9 hours later with waves up to 14 m (46 ft). 159 people killed, 488 injured, downtown Hilo devastated. Led to the establishment of the Pacific Tsunami Warning Center (PTWC) in Ewa Beach, Oahu.

Today's warnings: PTWC uses seismometers, ocean buoys (DART buoys), and tide gauges to detect and predict tsunamis. Hawaii's civil defense sirens and emergency alerts (phone) provide warning time to evacuate coastal areas.

Explain how OCEAN CURRENTS work. What drives surface currents? What drives deep ocean currents (thermohaline circulation)? How do currents affect Hawaii's climate and marine life?
Surface currents are driven by wind (trade winds and westerlies push surface water in consistent patterns). Earth's rotation (Coriolis effect) deflects currents to the right in the Northern Hemisphere, creating circular ocean gyres. The North Pacific Gyre circulates clockwise, including the North Equatorial Current that flows toward Hawaii from the east.

Deep ocean currents (thermohaline circulation) are driven by differences in water temperature and salinity (density). Cold, salty water sinks near the poles and flows along the ocean floor toward the tropics. This "ocean conveyor belt" circulates water through all ocean basins over ~1,000 years and distributes heat globally.

Effects on Hawaii:
  • Warm equatorial currents keep Hawaii's waters warm (24–28°C), supporting coral reefs
  • The North Pacific Current system influences weather patterns — El Niño (warm ENSO phase) increases hurricane risk; La Niña decreases it
  • Currents bring nutrients upwelling from deep water, supporting fish populations that Hawaiians and modern fisheries depend on

Unit 5: Earth's History and Fossils

⏱ 8 Weeks 📋 HCPS III: SC.6.6.2–SC.6.6.3 | NGSS: MS-ESS1-4, MS-ESS2-3

Earth is 4.54 billion years old. The rock beneath your feet has a story stretching back billions of years — written in layers of stone, preserved in fossils, decoded by scientists using radioactive clocks. In this final unit, we learn to read Earth's history — and reflect on what it means for our place in the story of creation.

📅 Relative Dating: Reading Rock Layers

Before absolute dating methods existed, geologists used relative dating to determine the sequence of geological events — which came first, which came later — using key principles:

  • Law of Superposition: In undisturbed rock layers (strata), older layers are on the BOTTOM; younger layers are on TOP. (Each new layer was deposited on top of existing layers.)
  • Law of Original Horizontality: Rock layers are originally deposited horizontally; tilted or folded layers were disturbed after deposition.
  • Cross-cutting Relationships: A fault or igneous intrusion that cuts through rock layers is YOUNGER than the layers it cuts.
  • Index Fossils: Fossils of organisms that lived for a short time but were widespread can identify specific time periods in rock layers worldwide.
🌋

Hawaiʻi Connection: The Hawaiian Islands show relative age beautifully — the rock layers of the Big Island are almost entirely young basalt (some only decades old from Kilauea eruptions). Kauaʻi's rocks are deeply weathered, with red laterite soils and dramatically eroded Na Pali cliffs — evidence of 5 million years of weathering. You can read the age of an island in its landscape.

⏱️ Absolute Dating: Radioactive Clocks

Radiometric (absolute) dating uses the predictable decay of radioactive isotopes to calculate the age of rocks and fossils in actual years.

Key concept: Half-life — the time it takes for half of a radioactive isotope to decay into a more stable element. Half-lives are constant and unaffected by temperature, pressure, or chemical state.

IsotopeHalf-lifeUseful for dating
Carbon-14 (¹⁴C)5,730 yearsOrganic materials up to ~50,000 years old
Potassium-40 (⁴⁰K)1.25 billion yearsVolcanic rocks; used extensively in Hawaii
Uranium-238 (²³⁸U)4.5 billion yearsVery old rocks; age of Earth itself

Note: Carbon-14 only works on once-living organisms (organic material). To date Hawaiian volcanic rocks, scientists use Potassium-40, which decays to Argon-40 — confirming that Kauaʻi's lava is ~5 million years old and the Big Island's oldest rocks are ~500,000 years old.

🦕 Fossils

A fossil is the preserved remains, impression, or trace of an ancient organism. Most fossils form in sedimentary rock. Types of fossil preservation:

  • Permineralization: Minerals in groundwater slowly replace original organic material (most common; e.g., petrified wood, dinosaur bones)
  • Mold fossil: The original organism decays, leaving a hollow impression in rock (like a footprint mold)
  • Cast fossil: Minerals fill a mold, creating a 3D replica of the original organism
  • Trace fossil: Preserved evidence of behavior — footprints, burrows, bite marks, eggs
  • Amber preservation: Organism trapped in tree resin that hardens — exceptional soft tissue preservation (insects, small lizards)

Note on Hawaii: Because most of Hawaii is made of young volcanic rock (where fossils rarely form), Hawaii has very few traditional fossils. However, paleontologists have found fossils of extinct Hawaiian birds (including giant flightless geese and eagles) preserved in lava tubes and caves.

❄️ Ice Ages and Natural Resources

The Pleistocene Epoch (2.6 million–11,700 years ago) was characterized by repeated glacial advances covering much of North America, Europe, and Asia. At the Last Glacial Maximum (~20,000 years ago), sea levels were ~120 m (400 ft) lower than today — exposing land bridges that allowed human migration. Hawaii was physically larger, and the Pacific's island chains were more connected.

Natural resources are materials from Earth that humans use. Renewable resources can replenish on human timescales (solar, wind, water, forests). Nonrenewable resources form over millions of years and cannot be replaced (coal, oil, natural gas, most minerals). Inexhaustible resources cannot be used up (sunlight, wind). Hawaii is transitioning toward 100% renewable energy by 2045 — one of the most ambitious clean energy goals in the US.

✝️ Faith Reflection: Deep Time and Human Dignity

Learning that Earth is 4.54 billion years old can feel overwhelming — where do we fit in this vast story? The Catholic tradition offers a profound answer: even in deep time, every human being has unique and infinite dignity.

The Catholic Church accepts the scientific age of Earth and the universe. The Vatican Observatory (one of the world's oldest astronomical institutions) actively conducts research. Pope Pius XII, Pope John Paul II, and Pope Francis have all affirmed that science and faith address different questions — how vs. why — and are not in conflict.

Hawaiian oral traditions (moʻolelo) preserve knowledge of past volcanic events, climate shifts, and astronomical observations across generations. Scientists have found that some Hawaiian stories correspond to actual geological events documented in rock layers. This shows that indigenous knowledge and Western science are not opponents — they are partners in understanding creation.

Whether we look at 4.5 billion years of Earth's history or the miracle of a single human cell, the Catholic response is the same: wonder, gratitude, and responsibility.

Unit 6: Plate Tectonics & Rates

⏱ 3 Weeks 📋 NGSS: MS-ESS2-2 | HCPS III: SC.6.6.1

How fast does a continent move? How do we measure something that takes millions of years? In this unit we apply rate calculations — a fundamental math skill — to understand how plate tectonics reshapes Earth over geological time. Math and geology work together to reveal the pace of our planet's slow dance.

📐 Rate = Distance ÷ Time

A rate is how much something changes per unit of time. Speed, for example, is distance ÷ time. In plate tectonics, we measure how many centimeters (or kilometers) a plate moves per year (or per million years).

Key Formula: Rate = Distance ÷ Time  |  Distance = Rate × Time  |  Time = Distance ÷ Rate

🔄 Unit Conversions in Geology

Plate speeds are tiny (cm/yr) but geological time is enormous (millions of years), so the result — distances moved — can be huge. Converting between units requires careful arithmetic:

  • 1 km = 1,000 m = 100,000 cm = 1,000,000 mm
  • 1 million years (Ma) = 1,000,000 years
  • Rate in cm/yr × 1,000,000 yr/Ma ÷ 100,000 cm/km = Rate in km/Ma × 10

Worked Example: The Pacific Plate moves at 7 cm/yr. How many km has it moved in 50 million years?
Distance = 7 cm/yr × 50,000,000 yr = 350,000,000 cm ÷ 100,000 cm/km = 3,500 km

🌍 Pangaea and Geological Time

About 200–300 million years ago, all landmasses formed a single supercontinent called Pangaea. Since then, plates have been separating. The Atlantic Ocean has been widening at ~2.5 cm/yr for about 200 million years:

Width = 2.5 cm/yr × 200,000,000 yr = 500,000,000 cm = 5,000 km — about the width of the Atlantic!

This match between calculation and observation is powerful evidence for plate tectonics. Alfred Wegener noticed that South America and Africa fit together like puzzle pieces and proposed continental drift in 1912 — but wasn't believed until GPS and seafloor spreading measurements confirmed plate motion decades later.

📏 Proportional Reasoning with Geologic Maps

Maps use scale to represent large distances in small spaces. A map scale of 1:1,000,000 means 1 cm on the map = 1,000,000 cm = 10 km in reality.

Example: On a 1:2,000,000 scale map, the Hawaiian chain measures 50 cm. Real distance = 50 cm × 2,000,000 = 100,000,000 cm = 1,000 km.

Proportional reasoning: if 1 cm = 10 km, then 3.5 cm = 35 km. These scale calculations appear constantly in geology, geography, and navigation.

Unit 7: Richter Scale & Powers of 10

⏱ 2 Weeks 📋 NGSS: MS-ESS3-2 | HCPS III: SC.6.6.3

When a magnitude 7 earthquake strikes, it releases not twice, but 1,000 times more energy than a magnitude 5. How is that possible? The answer lies in powers of 10 — a mathematical concept that explains why earthquake magnitudes can be so deceptively dangerous.

📉 The Richter Scale: A Logarithmic Scale

The Richter scale (more precisely, the moment magnitude scale, Mw) measures earthquake energy on a logarithmic scale. On a logarithmic scale, each step up is a multiplication, not an addition:

Scale TypeStepsExample
LinearAdd equal amounts1, 2, 3, 4, 5 (+1 each)
Logarithmic (base 10)Multiply by 1010, 100, 1,000, 10,000 (×10 each)

For ground shaking: each +1 on the Richter scale = 10× more ground motion.

For energy released: each +1 magnitude = ~31.6× more energy (10^1.5 ≈ 31.6).

Formula: Shaking ratio = 10^(M₂ − M₁)    Energy ratio ≈ 31.6^(M₂ − M₁) = 10^(1.5 × difference)

⚡ Powers of 10 in Action

Example 1: How much more shaking does an M6 produce than an M4?
Difference = 6 − 4 = 2    Shaking ratio = 10² = 100×

Example 2: How much more energy does an M8 release than an M5?
Difference = 8 − 5 = 3    Energy ratio = 10^(1.5×3) = 10^4.5 ≈ 31,623×

🔢 Scientific Notation Review

Powers of 10 connect directly to scientific notation — writing very large or small numbers as a decimal × 10^n:

  • 10² = 100 = 1.0 × 10²
  • 10⁶ = 1,000,000 = 1.0 × 10⁶
  • 3.16 × 10⁴ = 31,600
  • 6.5 × 10⁻³ = 0.0065

Scientists use scientific notation constantly because geology, astronomy, and physics deal with numbers ranging from subatomic (10⁻¹⁵ m) to galactic (10²⁶ m).

Unit 8: Radioactive Decay & Half-Life

⏱ 3 Weeks 📋 NGSS: MS-ESS1-4 | HCPS III: SC.6.6.4

How do geologists know that a Hawaiian lava flow is exactly 340,000 years old — not 200,000 or 500,000? The answer is radioactive decay: a clock embedded in every rock, ticking at a rate that never changes. The math behind radiometric dating is a beautiful application of exponential functions.

⚛️ What Is Radioactive Decay?

Unstable atomic nuclei (radioactive isotopes) spontaneously transform into different elements by emitting particles or energy. This process is random at the atomic level, but statistically predictable for large populations of atoms.

Key concept: half-life (t₁/₂) — the time it takes for exactly half of a sample of radioactive atoms to decay into the daughter element.

📐 The Half-Life Formula

After n half-lives, the fraction of original parent atoms remaining is:

N = N₀ × (1/2)ⁿ
where N = amount remaining, N₀ = original amount, n = number of half-lives elapsed

Example: Start with 800 g of Carbon-14 (half-life = 5,730 yr). How much remains after 3 half-lives (17,190 yr)?
N = 800 × (1/2)³ = 800 × 1/8 = 100 g

🌋 Dating Hawaiian Rocks with K-40

Carbon-14 cannot date volcanic rocks (no organic carbon). Instead, geologists use Potassium-40 (K-40), which is naturally present in basalt and decays to Argon-40 with a half-life of 1.25 billion years.

When lava solidifies, all previously accumulated Argon gas escapes. The rock's Ar-40 "clock" resets to zero. As time passes, K-40 decays and Ar-40 accumulates in the sealed rock. By measuring the K-40/Ar-40 ratio, scientists calculate the age.

🌋

Hawaiian Application: K-Ar dating confirms: Big Island oldest rocks ≈ 500,000 yr, Maui ≈ 1.3 Ma, Oahu ≈ 3.4 Ma, Kauaʻi ≈ 5 Ma. These ages perfectly match the expected ages from the Pacific Plate's movement rate over the hot spot!

The (1/2)ⁿ pattern appears in many real-world contexts beyond geology:

  • Medicine: Drug concentration in the bloodstream decreases by half every few hours
  • Finance: Value halving over time (similar to exponential decay)
  • Physics: Nuclear reactor fuel consumption
  • Electronics: Capacitor discharge follows the same pattern

Recognizing repeated halving as (1/2)ⁿ is a powerful mathematical tool across all sciences.

Unit 9: Topographic Maps & Slope

⏱ 3 Weeks 📋 NGSS: MS-ESS2-2 | HCPS III: SC.6.2.1

How does a flat map represent a three-dimensional mountain? How do geologists describe how steep a hillside is? The answers involve slope, contour lines, and map scale — mathematics that connects the abstract to the concrete landscape around us, including the dramatic volcanic peaks of Hawaiʻi.

🗺️ Contour Lines and Topographic Maps

A topographic map shows Earth's three-dimensional surface on a flat 2D map using contour lines — lines connecting points of equal elevation.

  • Contour interval: The elevation difference between adjacent contour lines (e.g., every line = 40 ft)
  • Index contours: Every 5th line, printed darker and labeled with elevation
  • Close lines = steep slope; Spread-out lines = gentle slope
  • V-shape pointing uphill = valley or stream; V-shape pointing downhill = ridge
  • Closed loops = hill summit; innermost loop = highest point
🌋

Mauna Kea Topo: Mauna Kea rises 4,205 m above sea level — the tallest mountain in the world measured from its base on the ocean floor (10,210 m total). A topographic map of Mauna Kea shows very close contour lines near the summit (steep slopes) and widely spaced lines lower down (gentle volcanic slopes typical of shield volcanoes).

📐 Slope = Rise ÷ Run

Slope measures steepness as the ratio of vertical change (rise) to horizontal distance (run):

Slope = Rise ÷ Run
Rise = change in elevation   Run = horizontal distance
Slope can be expressed as a fraction (1/4), decimal (0.25), percentage (25%), or angle (14°)

Hawaii Example: A trail on the Ko'olau Range rises 300 m over a horizontal distance of 1,500 m.
Slope = 300 ÷ 1,500 = 0.2 = 20% — a moderately steep trail.

📏 Map Scale and Real-World Distance

Map scale expresses the ratio between map distance and real-world distance:

  • Representative fraction (RF): 1:24,000 means 1 unit on map = 24,000 same units in reality. 1 cm = 24,000 cm = 240 m.
  • Bar scale: A drawn bar divided into labeled segments
  • Verbal scale: "1 inch = 1 mile"

Example: On a 1:50,000 map, two peaks are 7 cm apart.
Real distance = 7 × 50,000 = 350,000 cm = 3,500 m = 3.5 km

✏️ Reading Cross-Sections

A topographic cross-section (profile) shows what you would see if you sliced through the land vertically. To draw one:

  1. Draw a line across the topo map (the profile line)
  2. Where the profile line crosses each contour, mark that elevation on graph paper
  3. Connect the marks smoothly to show the terrain shape

Cross-sections help engineers design roads and tunnels, and geologists visualize underground structures.
