🌺 What Is Life Science?

Life Science (also called biology) is the branch of science that studies living organisms — how they are structured, how they function, how they interact with each other, and how they change over time. From the microscopic bacteria living in a drop of ocean water to the 100-foot-tall koa trees in Hawaiian rainforests, life science covers it all.

Life science includes many specialized fields:

  • Botany — the study of plants (including the iconic silversword of Haleakalā)
  • Zoology — the study of animals (including Hawaiian monk seals and nēnē geese)
  • Ecology — the study of how organisms interact with each other and their environment
  • Genetics — the study of heredity and DNA
  • Microbiology — the study of tiny organisms invisible to the naked eye

Hawaii is sometimes called the endangered species capital of the world — with more endangered species per square mile than anywhere in the United States. This makes studying life science here especially urgent and meaningful.

🧪 The Scientific Method

The scientific method is a systematic process scientists use to investigate questions about the natural world. It ensures that experiments are fair, repeatable, and trustworthy.

1
Observation
Notice something interesting in the natural world. Use your senses (and instruments) to gather information.
2
Question
Ask a specific, testable question about your observation. Example: "Does the amount of sunlight affect how tall a plant grows?"
3
Hypothesis
Make a testable prediction using IF-THEN format: "IF a plant receives more sunlight, THEN it will grow taller than a plant in shade."
4
Experiment
Design and conduct a controlled experiment. Change ONE variable at a time and keep all others constant.
5
Data Analysis
Organize, display, and analyze your results using data tables, graphs, and calculations.
6
Conclusion
Summarize what you found. Did the results support or refute your hypothesis? What did you learn? What new questions arise?

Notice that the scientific method is a cycle — conclusions lead to new observations and new questions. Science is never truly "finished."

📊 Hypothesis Formation and Experimental Design

A good hypothesis must be:

  • Testable — you can design an experiment to test it
  • Specific — it clearly states what you are testing
  • Falsifiable — it could potentially be proven wrong
  • Based on prior knowledge — grounded in what you already know

Every controlled experiment has these key parts:

  • Independent Variable (IV) — what the scientist deliberately changes (the "cause")
  • Dependent Variable (DV) — what the scientist measures; what changes in response to the IV (the "effect")
  • Controlled Variables — all other factors kept the same to make the test fair
  • Control Group — the group that receives NO experimental treatment (the baseline for comparison)
  • Experimental Group — the group that receives the experimental treatment

Example: Testing whether ocean water temperature affects coral bleaching in Hawaiian reefs:
• IV: Water temperature (25°C vs. 30°C)
• DV: Percentage of coral that bleaches
• Controlled variables: Type of coral, light level, water salinity, tank size
• Control group: Coral at normal ocean temperature (25°C)

Unit 2: Cells and Organisms

⏱ 8 Weeks 📋 HCPS III: SC.7.4.1–SC.7.4.2 | NGSS: MS-LS1-1–MS-LS1-3

Every living thing — from a coral polyp on a Hawaiian reef to a koa tree in a Mānoa rainforest to you — is built from the same fundamental unit: the cell. In this unit, we explore the incredible world inside cells and how they organize to form the complex organisms we see around us.

🔭 Cell Theory: The Foundation of Biology

The Cell Theory is one of the most fundamental principles in all of biology. It has three parts:

  1. All living things are made of one or more cells.
  2. The cell is the basic unit of structure and function in living things.
  3. All cells come from existing cells (cells don't spontaneously appear).

Cell Theory developed over centuries of scientific work. Robert Hooke (1665) was the first to observe cells — he saw tiny box-like chambers in cork and called them "cells." Anton van Leeuwenhoek (1670s) built better microscopes and discovered single-celled organisms in pond water. Matthias Schleiden (1838) concluded all plants are made of cells; Theodor Schwann (1839) said the same for animals. Rudolf Virchow (1855) added that all cells come from existing cells.

🏗️ Levels of Organization

In multicellular organisms, cells don't work alone. They organize into increasingly complex structures:

1
Cell
The basic unit of life. Example: muscle cell, nerve cell, red blood cell.
2
Tissue
Groups of similar cells working together. Example: muscle tissue, nerve tissue.
3
Organ
Groups of tissues working together. Example: heart, lungs, stomach.
4
Organ System
Groups of organs working together. Example: digestive system, circulatory system.
5
Organism
All organ systems working together as one living being. Example: you!
🪸

Hawaiʻi Connection: A coral reef shows a similar organizational principle. Individual coral polyp cells → tissue of one polyp → colonies of thousands of polyps → the coral reef structure → the entire reef ecosystem. Each level is more complex than the last!

The human body has 11 major organ systems: skeletal, muscular, nervous, endocrine, cardiovascular, lymphatic, respiratory, digestive, excretory, reproductive, and integumentary (skin). Each system interacts with others — you cannot separate them.

🔄 Cell Division and Growth

Mitosis is the process by which a cell divides to produce two identical daughter cells. This is how your body grows, replaces worn-out cells, and heals injuries. Your body produces approximately 3.8 million new cells every second through mitosis!

The cell cycle has key phases: Interphase (cell grows and copies its DNA), Mitosis (nucleus divides), and Cytokinesis (cytoplasm divides, producing two cells). Each new cell gets an exact copy of the original cell's DNA.

✝️ Faith Reflection: God's Design in Living Things

The more closely we study cells, the more astounding they become. A single human cell contains approximately 6 feet of DNA coiled incredibly tightly within a nucleus barely 6 micrometers wide. That DNA contains instructions more complex than the world's largest library. Each cell performs thousands of chemical reactions per second with astonishing precision.

The Catholic tradition sees this complexity not as evidence against God, but as a reflection of God's creative genius. Pope Francis writes: "The universe unfolds in God, who fills it completely. Hence, there is a mystical meaning to be found in a leaf, in a mountain trail, in a dewdrop, in a poor person's face."

As you study cell biology, allow yourself to experience wonder. The Catholic tradition calls this admiratio — a sense of awe that opens the mind to truth. The incredible complexity of even the simplest cell invites us to ask deeper questions about life, purpose, and the Creator.

Question 9
Explain the difference between MITOSIS and the reason cells divide. Why is cell division important for growth and repair?
Mitosis is the process of cell division in which one parent cell divides to produce two genetically identical daughter cells. Each daughter cell receives an exact copy of the parent cell's DNA (same number of chromosomes).

Reasons cells divide:
  1. Growth — as organisms grow, they need more cells (not bigger cells)
  2. Repair/replacement — worn-out, damaged, or dead cells must be replaced (e.g., skin cells replace themselves every ~2–4 weeks)
  3. Reproduction — some single-celled organisms reproduce by dividing
Note: Meiosis (not mitosis) produces sex cells with half the chromosome number — this is different from mitosis.

Unit 3: Genetics and Heredity

⏱ 8 Weeks 📋 HCPS III: SC.7.5.1–SC.7.5.2 | NGSS: MS-LS3-1–MS-LS3-2

Inside every cell in your body lies a molecule so important that it holds the instructions for building every living thing on Earth. That molecule is DNA. In this unit, we'll explore how traits are inherited from parents, how DNA works, and why no two living things are genetically identical — and what this means through the lens of faith.

🧬 DNA: The Blueprint of Life

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is a long, double-stranded molecule that carries the genetic instructions for the structure and function of all living organisms. Think of it as the world's most sophisticated instruction manual — written in a 4-letter chemical alphabet.

Key features of DNA:

  • Shape: Double helix — like a twisted ladder
  • The "rails" of the ladder: alternating sugar (deoxyribose) and phosphate groups
  • The "rungs" of the ladder: pairs of nitrogen bases
  • Four bases: Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C)
  • Base pairing rules: A always pairs with T; G always pairs with C

The human genome — the complete set of DNA instructions in one human cell — contains approximately 3 billion base pairs and about 20,000–25,000 genes. If you uncoiled all the DNA from just one cell and laid it end to end, it would stretch over 6 feet (2 meters)!

🐦

Hawaiʻi Connection: Scientists have sequenced the DNA of Hawaiian honeycreepers to trace their evolution. By comparing DNA sequences, researchers confirmed that all 50+ species evolved from a single rosefinch ancestor that arrived in Hawaii approximately 5–7 million years ago.

🔬 Chromosomes and Genes

DNA doesn't float loosely inside the nucleus — it's organized into structures called chromosomes. Chromosomes are tightly coiled DNA wrapped around proteins called histones.

  • Humans have 46 chromosomes in 23 pairs in most body cells
  • One chromosome in each pair comes from your mother; one from your father
  • Sex chromosomes: females have XX; males have XY
  • Chromosomes 1–22 are called autosomes; pair 23 are sex chromosomes

A gene is a specific segment of DNA that codes for a protein or trait. You have two copies of most genes — one from each parent. These alternate forms of a gene are called alleles.

🫛 Gregor Mendel and the Laws of Heredity

Gregor Mendel (1822–1884) was an Augustinian friar who discovered the basic principles of heredity by conducting careful experiments on pea plants in his monastery garden in what is now the Czech Republic. He spent 8 years crossbreeding thousands of plants and analyzing patterns in their traits.

Mendel chose pea plants because they have easily observable traits (seed color, pod shape, plant height), they can self-pollinate or cross-pollinate, and they reproduce quickly. His two key discoveries:

  • Law of Segregation: Each organism has two alleles for each trait; these separate during gamete (egg/sperm) formation, so each gamete gets only ONE allele.
  • Law of Independent Assortment: Genes for different traits are inherited independently of each other (for genes on different chromosomes).

📐 Punnett Squares

A Punnett square is a diagram that predicts the possible genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a genetic cross.

Example: Cross two heterozygous tall pea plants (Tt × Tt). T = tall (dominant), t = short (recessive).

Set up: Write one parent's alleles across the top (T, t) and the other parent's alleles down the side (T, t). Fill in the boxes.

Results: TT (25%) + Tt (50%) + tt (25%) = 3 tall : 1 short ratio

Probability: 75% of offspring show tall phenotype; 25% show short phenotype.

Punnett squares tell us probability, not certainty. If you flip a coin 4 times, you don't always get exactly 2 heads and 2 tails — but over many flips, you approach 50/50.

🌈 Genetic Variation

Genetic variation — the differences in DNA between individuals of the same species — is essential for evolution and a species' ability to survive changing environments.

Sources of genetic variation:

  • Mutations: Changes in DNA sequence (can be caused by UV radiation, chemicals, or copying errors). Most are neutral; some are harmful; rarely, some are beneficial.
  • Sexual reproduction: The shuffle of alleles during meiosis and fertilization creates unique combinations in every offspring.
  • Crossing over: During meiosis, chromosomes exchange segments, creating new allele combinations.
🐦

Hawaiʻi Connection: Hawaiian Honeycreepers are one of evolution's most spectacular examples of genetic diversification. Over millions of years, mutations and natural selection in an isolated island environment produced over 50 species — each with a unique beak shape adapted to a specific food source (curved beaks for nectar, thick beaks for seeds, long beaks for insects in bark). Sadly, more than half of these species are now extinct due to introduced diseases and habitat loss.

✝️ Faith Reflection: Diversity as Part of God's Plan

Genetics reveals something profound: no two humans who have ever lived share identical DNA (except identical twins). Out of the approximately 8 billion people alive today — and all the billions who have come before — each one is genetically unique. Your specific combination of alleles has never existed before and will never exist again.

The Catholic tradition affirms that every person is uniquely and intentionally created. Psalm 139:13–14 proclaims: "For you created my inmost being; you knit me together in my mother's womb. I praise you because I am fearfully and wonderfully made."

This genetic uniqueness extends to all of life. The incredible biodiversity of Hawaiian species — each with unique DNA finely tuned for its environment — reflects the extravagant creativity of God. As we learn genetics, we also encounter deep ethical responsibilities: genetic technology (like gene editing) requires careful moral discernment to ensure it serves human dignity and the common good.

Unit 4: Evolution, Natural Selection & Biodiversity

⏱ 8 Weeks 📋 HCPS III: SC.7.5.3–SC.7.5.4 | NGSS: MS-LS4-1–MS-LS4-6

How did the extraordinary diversity of life on Earth come to be? How did the Hawaiian silversword plant come to survive on a barren volcanic mountaintop? How did hundreds of unique species evolve on islands in the middle of the Pacific? In this unit, we explore natural selection, adaptation, and the rich biodiversity of our planet — including Hawaii's remarkable and endangered native species.

⛵ Darwin and Natural Selection

Charles Darwin (1809–1882) was a British naturalist who, after a 5-year voyage around the world on the HMS Beagle, developed the theory of natural selection. His most important observations came from the Galápagos Islands, where he noticed that each island had its own unique species of finch — each with a beak perfectly suited to its food source.

Darwin's theory of natural selection is based on four key principles:

  1. Variation: Individuals within a population show natural variation in their traits (size, color, beak shape, speed).
  2. Heritability: Many of these variations are heritable — passed from parent to offspring through genes.
  3. Selection: Some variations give individuals an advantage in surviving and reproducing in their environment. These individuals survive and leave more offspring.
  4. Time: Over many generations, helpful traits become more common in the population; unhelpful traits become rarer or disappear.

In short: Organisms with helpful traits survive, reproduce, and pass on those traits. Over time, populations change.

🦴 Evidence for Evolution

Scientists support evolutionary theory with multiple types of evidence:

  • Fossil Record: Fossils show a progression of life forms over millions of years. Transitional fossils show intermediate stages between ancestral and modern species.
  • Comparative Anatomy: Homologous structures — similar bone arrangements in different species (human arm, whale flipper, bat wing, horse leg) — suggest common ancestry. Analogous structures (bird wing and insect wing) evolved independently to serve similar functions.
  • Molecular Evidence: Comparing DNA sequences between species — the more similar the DNA, the more closely related the species. Humans and chimpanzees share approximately 98.7% of their DNA.
  • Biogeography: The geographic distribution of species matches evolutionary predictions. Island species closely resemble mainland species from nearby continents.

🌿 Adaptation in Hawaiian Species

An adaptation is any heritable trait that increases an organism's ability to survive and reproduce in its environment. Adaptations can be structural (physical features), behavioral (actions), or physiological (internal processes).

🌺

Silversword Plant (ʻĀhinahina): Found only on the rocky, windswept slopes of Haleakalā volcano on Maui at elevations of 6,900–10,000 feet, the silversword has remarkable adaptations: its silver, sword-shaped leaves are covered in tiny reflective hairs that deflect intense sunlight and trap moisture; its rosette shape channels rain to the root; it can live 50+ years before flowering once and dying. The silversword is considered sacred by Native Hawaiians.

🦢

Nēnē (Hawaiian Goose): Hawaii's state bird evolved from Canada geese. Unlike its webbed-footed relatives, the nēnē has reduced toe webbing and strong, padded feet — adaptations for walking on rough lava fields. It lives far from water, unlike most geese, nesting on volcanic hillsides.

🦭

Hawaiian Monk Seal (ʻĪlioholoikauaua): One of the most endangered marine mammals on Earth. Found only in Hawaii, this seal has adapted to warm tropical waters (unusual for seals) and feeds in deep coral reef environments. Its name in Hawaiian means "dog that runs in rough water."

🌴 Island Biodiversity and Adaptive Radiation

Hawaii's geographic isolation — the most remote island chain on Earth — created the perfect conditions for adaptive radiation: the rapid evolution of many species from one ancestor to fill different ecological niches.

How species form (speciation): When a population becomes geographically isolated (by an ocean, mountain range, or distance), the separated groups experience different environments, different random mutations, and different selection pressures. Over thousands of generations, they diverge genetically until they can no longer interbreed — they have become different species.

Hawaii's unique biodiversity includes: 1,000+ species of flowering plants (90% found nowhere else), 60+ species of native birds (though over half are now extinct or endangered), and the Hawaiian honeycreepers — 50+ species evolved from one ancestor.

⚠️ Biodiversity Crisis in Hawaii

Hawaii is sometimes called the "extinction capital of the world" — it has lost more species than any other US state. The causes include:

  • Habitat loss: Over 90% of Hawaiian lowland dry forests have been destroyed by agriculture and development
  • Invasive species: Introduced rats, mongoose, feral pigs, goats, and invasive plants devastate native species that evolved without these threats
  • Disease: Avian malaria (carried by introduced mosquitoes) has wiped out native birds from low elevations
  • Climate change: Rising temperatures push mosquitoes higher up mountains, threatening the last remaining disease-free bird habitats

Conservation efforts include: fencing out invasive animals, seed banking, captive breeding programs, and mosquito control using Wolbachia bacteria.

✝️ Faith Reflection: The Catholic Perspective on Evolution

The Catholic Church does not teach that evolution and faith are incompatible. In 1996, Pope John Paul II stated that evolution is "more than a hypothesis" and is compatible with Catholic faith, provided it is properly understood. The Church accepts that God could work through natural processes like evolution to bring forth the diversity of life.

This view — called theistic evolution — holds that God is the Creator who works through natural processes. Evolution describes the how of life's diversity; faith addresses the why — that creation has meaning, purpose, and value in God's eyes.

Pope Francis in Laudato Si (2015) emphasizes our responsibility to protect biodiversity: "Each year sees the disappearance of thousands of plant and animal species which we will never know, which our children will never see, because they have been lost for ever. The great majority become extinct for reasons related to human activity. Because of us, thousands of species will no longer give glory to God by their very existence."

Protecting Hawaiian species — the silversword, the nēnē, the honeycreepers — is not just an environmental issue. It is a moral responsibility, an act of mālama ʻāina and Catholic stewardship of God's creation.

Question 4
Why does Hawaii have such UNIQUE biodiversity? Explain the role of geographic isolation in species evolution. How is this similar to the Galápagos Islands that Darwin studied?
Why Hawaii's biodiversity is unique:
  • Hawaii is the most geographically isolated island chain on Earth — over 2,400 miles from the nearest continent
  • Species that arrived (by wind, water currents, or birds) were completely cut off from their mainland populations
  • They faced entirely new environments with different food sources, predators, and conditions
  • Random mutations and natural selection shaped populations differently on each island
  • Over millions of years, isolated populations became so genetically distinct they could no longer interbreed — forming new species
Geographic isolation role: When a population is separated from its parent population (by ocean, mountains, or distance), it experiences different selection pressures and accumulates different mutations. Over time, the two populations diverge genetically until they become separate species (speciation).

Galápagos comparison: Like Hawaii, the Galápagos Islands are isolated in the Pacific. Darwin observed 15 species of finches that all evolved from one South American ancestor — each adapted to a different food source on different islands, just like Hawaiian honeycreepers.

Unit 5: Ecosystems and Interactions

⏱ 8 Weeks 📋 HCPS III: SC.7.3.1–SC.7.3.2 | NGSS: MS-LS2-1–MS-LS2-4

No organism lives in isolation. Every living thing exists within a web of relationships — with other organisms, with the land, the water, and the air. In this final unit, we explore ecosystems: how energy flows, how organisms interact, and how we are called to be responsible stewards of these extraordinary communities of life that God has entrusted to us.

🌍 What Is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is all the living organisms (biotic factors) in an area, together with the nonliving (abiotic) environment they interact with. Ecosystems can be as small as a tide pool or as large as the entire Pacific Ocean.

Biotic Factors (living)Abiotic Factors (nonliving)
Plants, algae, coral polypsSunlight, temperature
Fish, sea turtles, dolphinsWater, salinity, pH
Bacteria, fungiSoil, rock, sand
Insects, birdsWind, rain, ocean currents

Organisms in an ecosystem are classified by their role:

  • Producers (autotrophs): Make their own food via photosynthesis (plants, algae, phytoplankton)
  • Consumers (heterotrophs): Must eat other organisms to get energy
  • Decomposers: Break down dead organic matter, recycling nutrients back into the ecosystem (bacteria, fungi)
🌊

Hawaiʻi Connection: Hawaii has extraordinary ecosystem diversity within a small area: coral reefs, sandy beaches, mangrove forests, lowland rainforests, montane bogs, alpine desert (atop Mauna Kea), and volcanic lava fields — each with its own unique community of organisms.

🐠 Food Chains and Food Webs

A food chain shows a simple, linear pathway of energy transfer: who eats whom. A food web shows the complex, interconnected reality of feeding relationships in an ecosystem.

Hawaiian Coral Reef Food Chain example:
Phytoplankton → Zooplankton → Triggerfish → Reef Shark

In food chains, arrows show the direction of energy flow (from eaten to eater). Each organism occupies a trophic level:

  • 1st trophic level: Producers (coral algae, phytoplankton, seagrass)
  • 2nd trophic level: Primary consumers / herbivores (sea urchins, parrotfish, surgeonfish)
  • 3rd trophic level: Secondary consumers (reef fish, octopus)
  • 4th trophic level: Tertiary consumers / apex predators (sharks, barracuda, Hawaiian monk seal)

⚡ Energy Flow and the 10% Rule

Energy enters most ecosystems from the sun through photosynthesis. As energy moves up the food chain, most of it is lost at each step — as heat from metabolism, movement, and waste.

The 10% Rule: Only about 10% of the energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next level. The other 90% is lost as heat.

This is why food chains rarely have more than 4–5 levels — there simply isn't enough energy left to support another trophic level. It also explains why it takes far more land/ocean to produce one pound of beef than one pound of grain.

An energy pyramid shows this — wide at the base (producers with the most energy) and narrow at the top (apex predators with least energy).

🌺 Hawaiian Ecosystems: Coral Reefs and Rainforests

Hawaiian Coral Reefs cover approximately 410,000 acres around the Hawaiian Islands and Northwestern Hawaiian Islands. They support over 7,000 species of marine life. Threats include: coral bleaching (from warm water), ocean acidification (from CO₂), overfishing, runoff pollution, and physical damage from boats and anchors.

Hawaiian Native Rainforests are dominated by ʻōhiʻa lehua trees and tree ferns (hāpuʻu). They support native honeycreepers, Hawaiian hoary bats (ōpeʻapeʻa), Hawaiian petrels, and thousands of unique invertebrates. The greatest threat is Rapid ʻŌhiʻa Death (ROD) — a fungal disease that has killed hundreds of thousands of ʻōhiʻa trees on Hawaiʻi Island since 2014.

Ahupuaʻa: Traditional Hawaiian land management divided land into wedge-shaped sections (ahupuaʻa) running from mountain peaks to the ocean. This ensured each community had access to mountain forests, agricultural lands, fishponds, and ocean fisheries. It reflected a deep understanding of ecosystem connectivity — that what happens in the mountains affects the coast, and vice versa.

Question 5
What is the CARBON CYCLE? Describe how carbon moves through a Hawaiian coral reef ecosystem, including the roles of photosynthesis, respiration, and decomposition.
Carbon Cycle: The continuous movement of carbon atoms through Earth's systems — atmosphere, living organisms, oceans, and soil — via biological and geological processes.

In a Hawaiian coral reef:
  1. Photosynthesis (carbon uptake): Zooxanthellae algae inside coral and reef algae absorb CO₂ from seawater to produce glucose. This removes carbon from the water.
  2. Respiration (carbon release): All reef organisms (coral, fish, bacteria) release CO₂ back into the water through cellular respiration as they break down glucose for energy.
  3. Consumption (carbon transfer): When fish eat algae or coral, carbon compounds move up the food chain.
  4. Decomposition: When organisms die, bacteria break down their tissues, releasing CO₂ back into the water.
  5. Calcification: Coral polyps absorb dissolved carbon (bicarbonate) from seawater to build their calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) skeletons — storing carbon long-term in coral rock.
  6. Ocean-atmosphere exchange: CO₂ moves between ocean surface and atmosphere; elevated atmospheric CO₂ increases ocean acidity (ocean acidification).
Question 6
Hawaii's coral reefs are threatened by OCEAN ACIDIFICATION caused by increased CO₂ in the atmosphere. Explain the cause of ocean acidification, why it harms corals, and what can be done to address it.
Cause of ocean acidification:
  1. Human activities (burning fossil fuels, deforestation) release excess CO₂ into the atmosphere
  2. Oceans absorb approximately 25–30% of this CO₂
  3. CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid) → lowers ocean pH (increases acidity)
  4. Ocean pH has dropped from ~8.2 to ~8.1 since industrialization — a 30% increase in acidity
Why it harms coral:
  • More acidic water makes it harder for coral to build and maintain their calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) skeletons
  • At lower pH, calcium carbonate actually dissolves — existing coral skeletons weaken and erode
  • Combined with coral bleaching (from warm water), acidification threatens reef survival globally
Solutions:
  • Reduce CO₂ emissions: switch to renewable energy, reduce fossil fuel burning
  • Protect reef health: reduce other stressors (pollution, overfishing) so reefs are more resilient
  • Marine protected areas: limit human disturbance in key reef zones
  • Coral gardening: grow resilient coral fragments and transplant them to bleached areas

Unit 6: Population Growth Models

⏱ 3 Weeks 📋 NGSS: MS-LS2-1 | HCPS III: SC.7.3.1

How does a single pair of rabbits become thousands in just a few years? Why did the Hawaiian monk seal almost go extinct, and how are scientists tracking its recovery? Population biology is driven by math — specifically, exponential growth and the limits that stop it.

📈 Exponential Growth

When a population has unlimited food, space, and no predators, it grows exponentially — doubling at regular intervals. The key number is the doubling time (td): how many years (or generations) for the population to double.

Formula: N = N₀ × 2^(t/td)
N = population at time t, N₀ = starting population, t = time elapsed, td = doubling time

Example: A bacterial colony of 100 cells doubles every 20 minutes. After 2 hours (120 min = 6 doublings):
N = 100 × 2⁶ = 100 × 64 = 6,400 cells

🔢 The Rule of 70

For any quantity growing at a constant percentage rate r, the doubling time is approximately:

Doubling time ≈ 70 ÷ r (where r is the growth rate in percent)

Example: A population growing at 2%/year → doubling time ≈ 70 ÷ 2 = 35 years

Growth RateDoubling Time (Rule of 70)
1%/year~70 years
2%/year~35 years
3.5%/year~20 years
7%/year~10 years
10%/year~7 years

📉 Logistic Growth and Carrying Capacity

Exponential growth cannot continue forever. Real populations are limited by food, space, predation, and disease — collectively called limiting factors. The maximum population an environment can sustain is the carrying capacity (K).

Real populations typically show logistic growth — an S-shaped curve that starts exponential, slows as it approaches K, and levels off. This is a more realistic model than pure exponential growth.

🌺

Hawaiian Monk Seal: The Hawaiian monk seal is critically endangered with ~1,600 individuals (2024). Growing at ~3%/year, its doubling time = 70 ÷ 3 ≈ 23 years. If this rate holds, the population would reach 3,200 by ~2047. Scientists monitor this using population models.

🐺 Predator-Prey Cycles

Predator and prey populations cycle together in a pattern described by the Lotka-Volterra model:

  1. Prey population grows → more food for predators
  2. Predator population grows → more prey eaten
  3. Prey population falls → predators run out of food
  4. Predator population falls → prey recovers → cycle repeats

Classic example: Canadian lynx and snowshoe hare populations cycle in approximately 10-year cycles. In Hawaii, invasive mongoose (predator) severely reduced native ground-nesting bird populations — disrupting the natural cycle.

✝️ Faith Reflection: Stewardship and the Balance of Life

Population mathematics shows us that ecosystems are delicately balanced — a small change in growth rate or carrying capacity can determine whether a species recovers or goes extinct. The Hawaiian monk seal's survival depends on human choices about fishing, pollution, and habitat protection.

Understanding population models is not just academic — it is essential to our moral responsibility as stewards of creation.

Unit 7: Genetics & Probability

⏱ 3 Weeks 📋 NGSS: MS-LS3-2 | HCPS III: SC.7.4.1

Why do some children have blue eyes when both parents have brown? How do genetic diseases get passed silently through families for generations? Genetics is fundamentally a science of probability — and Punnett squares are multiplication tables in disguise.

🧬 Punnett Squares as Multiplication Tables

A Punnett square predicts the probability of offspring having a particular genotype. For a monohybrid cross (one gene, two alleles), each parent contributes one allele. The probability of an offspring getting a specific allele pair equals the product of the individual probabilities.

Monohybrid Cross: Bb × Bb
B = dominant allele (brown fur); b = recessive (white fur)
Probability of BB = ½ × ½ = ¼  |  Bb = 2 × (½ × ½) = ½  |  bb = ½ × ½ = ¼
Genotype ratio: 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb
Phenotype ratio: 3 brown : 1 white (3:1)

The 3:1 ratio was discovered by Gregor Mendel in 1866 through pea plant experiments — one of the most important discoveries in biology.

📊 Probability Rules

Product Rule: The probability of two INDEPENDENT events both occurring = P(A) × P(B)

Sum Rule: The probability of EITHER of two mutually exclusive events = P(A) + P(B)

Example: In a Bb × Bb cross, what is the probability of a white offspring (bb)?
P(b from parent 1) = ½   P(b from parent 2) = ½
P(bb) = ½ × ½ = ¼ = 25%

🌺 Hawaiian Genetic Examples

Sickle cell trait in Pacific Islander populations: The sickle cell allele (Hb^S) is recessive. Carriers (Hb^A Hb^S) are largely unaffected but can pass the allele to children. Two carriers: 25% chance of sickle cell disease (Hb^S Hb^S). In populations where malaria was historically present (including some Pacific Island regions), the carrier state provides some protection — an example of balanced selection.

Hawaiian honeycreeper beak variation: Bill shape diversity in Hawaiian honeycreepers (ʻiʻiwi, ʻapapane, nēnē) evolved through natural selection acting on heritable variation — the raw material of which is genetic diversity maintained by Mendelian inheritance.

📐 Observed vs. Expected: Chi-Square Thinking

Mendel's 3:1 ratio is a probability prediction, not a guarantee. In small samples, results deviate from predictions by chance. If you cross 100 Bb × Bb plants, you expect 75 dominant and 25 recessive — but you might get 78 and 22.

Scientists use a chi-square test (χ²) to determine whether deviations from expected ratios are due to chance or represent a real biological difference. The key question: "Is this deviation what we'd expect from random sampling, or is something else happening?"

Simple check: Expected: 75 round, 25 wrinkled. Observed: 78 round, 22 wrinkled. Difference: 3 from each. For 100 plants, a difference of 3 is well within normal random variation.

Unit 8: Pharmacokinetics & Drug Half-Life

⏱ 2 Weeks 📋 NGSS: MS-PS1-5 | HCPS III: SC.7.1.2

When you take a pain reliever, where does it go? How long does it work? Why do doctors specify "every 6 hours"? The answers come from pharmacokinetics — the study of how drugs move through the body, governed by the same half-life mathematics as radioactive decay.

💊 Drug Absorption and Elimination

After you swallow a pill, the drug is absorbed into the bloodstream. The liver and kidneys then work to eliminate it. For many common drugs, elimination follows a half-life pattern: the concentration in blood drops by half in a predictable time period, regardless of the starting concentration.

Formula: C = C₀ × (1/2)^(t/t½)
C = concentration at time t, C₀ = initial concentration, t½ = drug half-life

Ibuprofen (Advil)~2 hoursEvery 6–8 hours

📊 Therapeutic Windows

Every drug has a therapeutic window — the range of blood concentration that is effective without being toxic:

  • Below MEC (Minimum Effective Concentration): Drug has no effect — concentration too low
  • Within therapeutic window: Drug works as intended
  • Above MTC (Minimum Toxic Concentration): Dangerous — side effects, overdose risk

Dosing intervals are designed to keep concentration within the therapeutic window — taking the next dose before levels drop below MEC, but not so frequently that levels exceed MTC.

🌺

Hawaiian Medicinal Plants: Traditional Hawaiian healers (kahuna lāʻau lapaʻau) understood dosing empirically. Plants like noni (Morinda citrifolia) were used for pain and infection. Modern research shows noni contains compounds with anti-inflammatory properties. Understanding half-life helps us see why traditional dosing frequencies (daily, twice daily) often correspond to pharmacokinetic reality.

🔢 Calculating Drug Levels Over Time

Example: 500 mg of acetaminophen (t½ = 2.5 hr) is taken at 8:00 AM. How much remains at 5:00 PM (9 hours later)?
Number of half-lives = 9 hr ÷ 2.5 hr = 3.6 half-lives
Amount remaining ≈ 500 × (1/2)^3.6 ≈ 500 × 0.082 ≈ 41 mg
Most of the dose has been eliminated in 9 hours.

For simplicity, we often use whole-number half-lives: after 3 half-lives (~7.5 hr), 500 × (1/2)³ = 500 × 1/8 = 62.5 mg remains — most of the therapeutic dose is gone.

Unit 9: Ecology & Biodiversity Index

⏱ 3 Weeks 📋 NGSS: MS-LS2-5 | HCPS III: SC.7.3.3

How do scientists measure whether an ecosystem is healthy? A forest with 1,000 trees all of one species is less biodiverse — and more fragile — than a forest with 200 trees each of 5 different species. Biodiversity indices use proportions and mathematics to quantify what "healthy" means.

🌿 Species Richness and Evenness

Species richness = the number of different species in an area (simple count).

Species evenness = how equally individuals are distributed among species.

A healthy ecosystem has BOTH high richness (many species) AND high evenness (balanced populations). An area dominated by one invasive species may have moderate richness but very low evenness — and is ecologically fragile.

Both communities have richness = 3, but Community 2 is far more evenly distributed — and more resilient.

📐 Simpson's Diversity Index

Simpson's Diversity Index (D) accounts for both richness and evenness. It measures the probability that two randomly selected individuals belong to DIFFERENT species:

D = 1 − Σ(nᵢ/N)²
nᵢ = number of individuals of species i
N = total number of individuals
D ranges from 0 (no diversity) to 1 (maximum diversity)

Step-by-step example: Community has Species A=50, B=30, C=20. Total N=100.
Proportion A: 50/100 = 0.5 → (0.5)² = 0.25
Proportion B: 30/100 = 0.3 → (0.3)² = 0.09
Proportion C: 20/100 = 0.2 → (0.2)² = 0.04
Σ(nᵢ/N)² = 0.25 + 0.09 + 0.04 = 0.38
D = 1 − 0.38 = 0.62 (moderately diverse)

🌺 Hawaiian Biodiversity and Invasive Species

Hawaii is the world's most isolated island chain — and the world's extinction capital. Over 70% of recorded US plant and bird extinctions have occurred in Hawaii. The main causes: invasive species (rats, mongoose, feral pigs, invasive plants) and habitat loss.

🌺

Native vs. Invasive: Hawaii's native rainforests once held hundreds of honeycreeper species; today ~50 remain, many critically endangered. Invasive strawberry guava (Psidium cattleianum) dominates many native forests, reducing species richness dramatically. A native ʻōhiʻa forest might have D = 0.85; an invaded guava forest might have D = 0.25 — a dramatic biodiversity loss captured in a single number.

🔢 Proportions in Ecology

Many ecological calculations use proportions — a species as a fraction of total community:

  • Relative abundance = nᵢ/N × 100%
  • Dominance = highest single species proportion
  • Invasive species impact = change in D before vs. after invasion

These calculations help scientists track ecosystem health over time — comparing D values measured in different years shows whether a restoration project is working.
